## The R90 Technique — Core Mechanism
> *Source: `transcripts/02_sleep_in_cycles_final.txt` · lines 7–43*

**P-018** [CONFIRMED — DOC-002]
Sleep has **4 stages**, each with a distinct purpose:
1. Light sleep — transitional, still aware of surroundings
2. Deeper sleep — body slows
3. Deep sleep — physical repair: muscle, bone, immune system
4. REM — brain consolidates memory, solves problems, drives creativity

**P-019** [CONFIRMED — DOC-002]
The body operates in natural **90-minute sleep cycles**. A 24-hour day contains exactly **16 × 90-minute cycles** — some for sleep, some for waking activity. This is the foundational unit of R90.

**P-020** [CONFIRMED — DOC-002]
Nick's framework is named the **R90 technique**. It turns 90-minute cycle science into a complete restructuring of the 24-hour day — not just night-time sleep.

**P-021** [CONFIRMED — DOC-002]
To find optimal sleep timing: **count backwards from desired wake time in 90-minute increments**. Waking at the end of a cycle (light stage) produces alertness; waking mid-cycle produces grogginess.

**P-022** [CONFIRMED — DOC-002]
The **post-sleep period** is a critical recovery phase. Sequenced, unhurried actions to reach full active wake state:
1. Empty the bladder
2. Move from darkness to daylight
3. Hydrate
4. Allow appetite and fuel up
5. Mental challenges
6. Exercise
7. Empty the bowels

**P-023** [CONFIRMED — DOC-002]
**Morning daylight exposure** triggers serotonin (the "get active" hormone) and resets the circadian clock — ensuring the body naturally reaches tiredness at the right time in the evening. It is described as "more important than you might think."

**P-024** [CONFIRMED — DOC-002]
**Worrying about sleep is the biggest disruptor of sleep.** Nick states this twice. Outcome anxiety is self-defeating. The R90 solution: focus on process variables, not sleep metrics.

**P-025** [CONFIRMED — DOC-002]
**Daytime brain load directly drives night sleep quality.** "Put [the brain] under pressure during your day and guess what, lower levels of sleep quality is what you get as a result." Day and night are inseparable in R90.

**P-026** [CONFIRMED — DOC-002]
The operational container for R90 is the **Sleep-Wake Routine (SWR)** — a structured daily framework. Without a structured SWR, recovery cannot be maximised.

## Formal R90 Terminology — Confirmed (DOC-003)
> *Source: `transcripts/03_sleep_recovery_final.txt` · lines 5, 15, 27, 29*

**P-041** [CONFIRMED — DOC-003]
**ARP = Anchor Reset Point.** The fixed, consistent daily wake-up and start time. The master anchor from which all 16 daily 90-minute cycles are organised. Prior inference of "Anchor Recovery Point" is incorrect.

**P-042** [CONFIRMED — DOC-003, PPT-001]
**MRM = Micro Reset Moment.** A 3–5 minute mental disengagement break taken every 90-minute waking cycle. Target: 7 MRMs per active day (Phases 1–3). No sleep required — vacant mindspace only. Even at minimum, measurably improves sleep onset, sleep maintenance, and deep sleep access. *(Earlier sources say "minimum 5 minutes" and "Micro Recovery Moment" — normalised to current preferred form per PPT-001.)*

**P-043** [CONFIRMED — DOC-003]
**Electronic Insomnia** — Nick's term for screen/gaming/social-media-driven sleep disruption that creates a "can't switch it off" mindset. Classified as a distinct and rapidly growing modern pathology, separate from conventional insomnia.

**P-044** [CONFIRMED — DOC-003]
**Random interventions in isolation are counterproductive.** Any single tool — mattress, tracker, app, supplement, meditation — applied without a structural framework can worsen sleep and promote more persistent insomnia. Structure (the SWR/16-cycle plan) must come first.

**P-045** [CONFIRMED — DOC-003]
**SWS (Slow Wave Sleep) = deep sleep.** During SWS: growth hormone is released. Longer SWS → better daytime wakefulness. SWS deprivation → increased daytime sleepiness + direct reduction in athletic performance.

**P-046** [CONFIRMED — DOC-003]
**Physical recovery during sleep is biochemically specific:** glycogen replenishment and protein synthesis (muscle repair/growth) depend on deep and REM sleep cycles. Without adequate cycles, training gains are negated regardless of workout quality.

## Pre/Post Routines — KSRI 4
> *Source: `transcripts/Lesson_4.txt` · lines 1–270 (key refs: staircase 89–122; polarity pairs 126–158; temperature 169–187)*

**P-047** [CONFIRMED — AUD-004]
**Post-sleep routine > pre-sleep routine.** The entire day is the preparation for sleep. Expecting to fix everything in the last 30 minutes before bed ignores 16 hours of accumulated conditions. The morning routine sets the trajectory for the next night's recovery.

**P-048** [CONFIRMED — AUD-004]
**The staircase metaphor.** Each 90-minute cycle is a descent: top = light sleep, bottom = deep/REM sleep. Only **20–25% of each cycle** is accessible as deep or REM sleep. Anything still processing (food, fluid, unresolved thoughts) traps you near the top — you recover, but far less than possible.

**P-049** [CONFIRMED — AUD-004]
**Two daily polarity pairs** govern the biological rhythm:
- **Morning (activation):** warm → cool, dark → light
- **Evening (deactivation):** light → dark, cool → warm
These mirror the sunup/sundown process from KSRI 1 and are the environmental triggers Nick's routines are built around.

**P-050** [CONFIRMED — AUD-004]
**Bedroom temperature rule:** Body temperature should be a couple of degrees warmer than the bedroom — not a hot room, not cold bedding. The differential triggers sleep onset. A quick shower or light exercise before bed achieves the necessary body warming.

**P-051** [CONFIRMED — AUD-004]
**Evening light protocol:** Shift progressively to amber, yellow, or red light as the evening advances. Blue/white light suppresses melatonin. Dim, warm light allows melatonin to build while remaining comfortably active.

**P-052** [CONFIRMED — AUD-004]
**Pre-sleep routine is personalisable** around the core light/temperature framework. Exercise, yoga, meditation, sounds, smells are optional additions — not replacements for the environmental conditions.

## Chronotype — KSRI 2
> *Source: `transcripts/Lesson_2.txt` · lines 1–86 (key refs: AMer/PMer 9–14, 50; mechanism 54–67; population split 31–33)*

**P-027** [CONFIRMED — AUD-002]
Two primary chronotypes:
- **AMer (morning / lark):** naturally wakes 5–7am; peak cognitive and physical performance in the morning; fades by evening
- **PMer (evening / owl):** ~1–2 hour phase delay; active and productive in late afternoon/evening; comfortable well into night

**P-028** [CONFIRMED — AUD-002]
Chronotype mechanism: the **speed of the melatonin-to-serotonin shift** in the pineal gland. AMers shift faster at sunrise; PMers lag by ~1–2 hours. This is genetic and innate — it cannot be changed, only aligned with or fought against.

**P-029** [CONFIRMED — AUD-002]
Population split (Nick's professional estimate): **~30% AMers / ~70% PMers.** The majority are living in an AMer-structured world (early starts, morning culture) that is chronobiologically mismatched to their genetics.

**P-030** [CONFIRMED — AUD-002]
A third emerging category: **"in-betweeners."** Lifestyle, occupation, and social habits have blurred the natural chronotype. These people camouflage with caffeine and stimulants. This is a distinct problematic pattern, not a true third type.

## Recovery Cycles — KSRI 3
> *Source: `transcripts/Lesson_3.txt` · lines 1–95 (key refs: 5 cycles 18–20; 35/week 37; CRP 64–65; wake time 80; 30% rule 51–52)*

**P-034** [CONFIRMED — AUD-003]
**"Eight hours" is a myth.** No one sleeps 8 hours straight through. The 90-minute cycle is the correct measurement unit. 5 × 90-min cycles = 7.5h — the R90 equivalent of "8 hours," but structured around cycle completion, not duration.

**P-035** [CONFIRMED — AUD-003]
**Weekly target: 35 cycles** (5/day × 7 days). This is the primary resilience metric — not nightly totals. A 3-cycle night is recoverable within the week. Anxiety is removed by managing at the weekly level.

**P-036** [CONFIRMED — AUD-003]
**The consistent wake time is the immovable anchor.** Sleep onset times flex; wake time never changes — not on weekends, not after short nights. "The sun doesn't change, so we can move the sleep times but not the wake time."

**P-037** [CONFIRMED — AUD-003, PPT-001]
**CRP (Controlled Reset Period):** a deliberate 30-minute mindspace rest, midday or in the early evening (Phase 2, cycles 6/7/8). Does not require sleep — mental disengagement is sufficient. Counts as a recovery cycle toward the 35/week target. Nick rebrands "napping" to remove cultural stigma: it is a professional recovery tool, not a weakness. *(Earlier sources say "20–30 min" and "Controlled Recovery Period" — normalised to current preferred form per PPT-001.)*

**P-038** [CONFIRMED — AUD-003]
**~30% of the 24-hour period in sleep state** is the R90 proportion target. 30% × 24h ≈ 7.2h ≈ 5 cycles. Sleep is a fraction of the total day, not a separate phase.

**P-039** [CONFIRMED — AUD-003]
**Every 90 minutes during waking hours:** take a micro-recovery break — a brief mental distraction. This is passive, lightweight, and requires no planning. It maintains the polyphasic recovery rhythm throughout the day.

**P-040** [CONFIRMED — AUD-003]
**The first 90-minute block after waking** (the post-sleep period) is an active recovery/activation cycle — not dead time. It has a defined sequence (per P-022) and directly impacts the quality of the entire day.

## Four Phases Framework (from DOC-006)
> *Source: `transcripts/R90-T_Playbook_Overview_.txt` · lines 280, 446, 600–606*

**P-053** [CONFIRMED — DOC-006]
**The 24-hour day is divided into four phases**, each spanning approximately 4 waking cycles or a block of nocturnal sleep:
- **Phase 1 (Sunrise → Midday):** Cycles 1–4. Activation, high-performance activity. MRMs applied each cycle.
- **Phase 2 (Midday → Early Evening):** Cycles 5–8. CRP window (cycles 6/7/8). Continued activity. MRMs applied.
- **Phase 3 (Evening → Sleep onset):** Cycles 8–11/12. Wind-down. Pre-sleep preparation. MRMs applied.
- **Phase 4 (Nocturnal sleep):** Cycles 11/12–16. Typically 5 sleeping cycles (3–6 possible). Deep/REM stages concentrated in first 3 cycles of Phase 4.

**P-054** [CONFIRMED — DOC-006]
**Worrying about sleep is its main disruptor** — this principle appears twice in DOC-006 as the document's lead statement. The entire R90 framework is designed to remove sleep anxiety by replacing outcome focus with process focus (cycles, phases, routines).

**P-055** [CONFIRMED — AUD-005]
**A CRP does not require sleep.** It is "mind space time, mindset" — a deliberate mental disengagement. Even walking outside and back in counts. The 30-minute window is the container; whether sleep occurs is secondary. This distinguishes CRP from napping and removes the "can't nap" barrier.

**P-056** [CONFIRMED — DOC-006]
**SleepING reframe.** Nick coaches clients to say "sleepING" (with ING) — repositioning it as an active daily health pillar equivalent to hydrating, eating, exercising, and socialising. The linguistic shift changes mindset from passive ("I need to get sleep") to proactive ("I am sleepING as part of my day").

**P-057** [CONFIRMED — DOC-006]
**Recovery = Activity** in importance. The 24-hour day must balance mental/physical activity with mental/physical recovery. Overloading activity without recovery causes burnout. "In every 24 hours, we want to see this better balance between activity and recovery to protect you from everything you eat and drink and exercise."

**P-058** [CONFIRMED — DOC-006]
**Sleep Noise** is a distinct environmental hazard. Algorithm-driven media constantly amplifies sleep anxiety ("you better get it right every night or else"). The R90 approach is explicitly positioned as an antidote — not another source of sleep pressure.

## Sleep Products and Physical Environment (from AUD-007, DOC-006)
> *Source: `transcripts/Lesson_7.txt` (AUD-007) · lines 1–237; `transcripts/R90-T_Playbook_Overview_.txt` (DOC-006) · lines 738–820*

**P-059** [CONFIRMED — AUD-007, DOC-006]
**Default sleeping position: foetal, non-dominant side.** Right-handers on left side; left-handers on right. Rationale: (1) non-dominant side = less joint/muscle pressure; (2) dominant side free = security signal to the brain, enabling deeper sleep states. This is the single most consistently coached physical positioning principle.

**P-060** [CONFIRMED — AUD-007]
**Pillows are a symptom of an inadequate mattress surface**, not an independent solution. If the mattress surface correctly releases the body in foetal position, no pillow (or only a minimal one) is needed. Standard pillows raise the neck, block airways, and contribute to mouth breathing, snoring, and neck/back pain.

**P-061** [CONFIRMED — AUD-007]
**Mattress layering** is the preferred approach: build the sleep surface in layers, not as a single rigid unit. Benefits: customisable, each layer replaceable, key layer is portable (athletes carry their layer to hotels). A layered surface allows the body to sink into foetal position without gap — eliminating the need for a pillow.

**P-062** [CONFIRMED — AUD-007]
**Individual duvets for sleeping partners** remove one of the most common and unacknowledged sleep disruptors — competing temperature preferences. Each person managing their own temperature differential independently is the preferred approach.

**P-063** [CONFIRMED — AUD-007]
**Nose breathing during sleep is the natural and optimal state.** Mouth breathing is prevalent but disruptive. It can be trained. Nasal breathing is associated with quieter sleep, better airway function, and deeper cycle access.

**P-064** [CONFIRMED — AUD-007]
**Air quality and hypoallergenic bedding** matter. Synthetic bedding often outperforms natural materials for this reason — it doesn't harbour dust mites. Frequent linen washing and room airing are baseline environmental maintenance.

**P-065** [CONFIRMED — AUD-007]
**Fresh bed linen is a sleep onset familiarisation trigger.** The cooler, fresher feel of freshly changed linen creates a conditioned signal that this is a good sleep environment. Effect diminishes significantly by night 2. Increasing change frequency is a direct lever for sleep quality perception.

**P-066** [CONFIRMED — DOC-006]
**Light targets for the 24-hour environment:**
- Target morning exposure: 10,000 Lux (natural daylight or light therapy device)
- Target per 90-min cycle in Phases 1 & 2: ~1,000 Lux
- Reference: ~80,000 Lux outdoors (clear day), ~3,000 Lux by a window, ~500 Lux in centre of room
- Seasonal daily ideal: ~12 hours daylight, ~4 hours diminished amber light, ~8 hours dark

**P-067** [CONFIRMED — DOC-006]
**The 15-minute rule:** if sleep onset has not occurred within 15 minutes of lying down, get up, remain in chillout mode, wait for the next 90-minute cycle boundary, and try again. Lying anxious in bed compounds the problem. The next cycle boundary is always the correct entry point.

**P-068** [CONFIRMED — PPT-001]
**Seasonal light targets vary significantly** and must be planned for. Reference targets per season (UK/Northern Europe baseline):
- **Standard day:** 12h daylight, 4h diminished amber, 8h dark
- **April–September:** 16h daylight, 2h diminished amber, 6h dark
- **October–March:** 8h daylight, 8h diminished amber, 8h dark → SAD risk window requiring active light therapy intervention

**P-069** [CONFIRMED — PPT-001]
**MRM specification:** 7 Micro Reset Moments per day — one per active waking cycle across Phases 1, 2, and 3. Duration: 3–5 minutes each. Total: 21–35 minutes of mindspace recovery distributed across the waking day. The target is "vacant mindspace" — not task-switching, not passive entertainment.

**P-070** [CONFIRMED — PPT-001]
**Gut health directly enables restorative sleep.** "Gut healthy helps your brain develop restorative sleep stages." Eating and hydrating slowly (taking twice as long as usual) is a named performance tactic — not just a wellness recommendation. A compromised gut creates physiological noise that disrupts the descent through sleep stages.

## Environment and Familiarisation (from AUD-006)
> *Source: `transcripts/Lesson_6.txt` · lines 1–248*

**P-071** [CONFIRMED — AUD-006]
**Unfamiliar environments reduce recovery by up to 40%** — mentally and physically. This is not a function of mattress quality or temperature; it is a function of the brain's failure to register the environment as familiar and safe. The security signal required for deep sleep entry is blocked. This is why athlete travel protocols are performance-critical.

**P-072** [CONFIRMED — AUD-006]
**Familiarisation is the core mechanism of environment.** The sleeping space must contain personal sensory cues that signal safety to the brain: sounds (noise profile matching upbringing), smells (essential oils, laundry products, nature references), visuals (calming, nature-themed, non-stimulating), and comfort objects (childhood items, personal anchors). These cues travel with the person to hotels, travel environments, extreme locations.

**P-073** [CONFIRMED — AUD-006]
**Blackout blinds and a Dawn Wake Simulator operate as a paired intervention.** Blackout alone prevents the natural serotonin trigger — it must be accompanied by an artificial sunrise (DWS) inside the room. Used independently, blackout worsens the morning activation problem it was installed to solve.

## Sleep Disorders and Recovery (from AUD-008)
> *Source: `transcripts/Lesson_8.txt` · lines 1–359*

**P-074** [CONFIRMED — AUD-008]
**The primary deep sleep window is 10pm–2am.** "Between sort of 10pm in the evening and 2am in the morning, within that natural process, is when the human is more likely to have achieved those deeper sleep stages. From the point of around 2 o'clock, 3 o'clock onwards... you're unlikely to be in those deeper sleep stages in general terms." The R90 framework structures nocturnal cycles to capture this window.

**P-075** [CONFIRMED — AUD-008]
**2–3am wakefulness is a polyphasic heritage pattern, not a disorder.** It occurs naturally at the transition out of the deep sleep phase. The correct response is: do not force re-entry; chill out; wait for the next cycle to present naturally. Anxiety at 2–3am compounds the problem; calm waiting resolves it.

**P-076** [CONFIRMED — AUD-008]
**Ortho-insomnia** is a new and growing modern disorder — sleep anxiety triggered by consumer tracker data. Trackers are a guide, not a definitive performance criterion. Sleep data should be used contextually alongside how the person feels, not as a primary judgement of sleep quality.

**P-077** [CONFIRMED — AUD-008]
**Sleep restriction** is a deliberate reset tactic for disrupted sleep patterns. Choose a later cycle entry time (e.g., shift from 23:00 to 00:30 or 02:00) to consolidate cycles and strengthen the circadian signal. Compensate with daytime CRPs. Do not try to accumulate hours — accumulate complete cycles at the correct circadian depth.

## Nutrition, Caffeine, and Meal Timing (from TXT-001)
> *Source: `Data Brut/Art de mieux dormir, L_ - Nick Littlehales.txt` · Chapters 2, 4, 5, 8 (TXT-001 — French edition of "Sleep")*

**P-078** [CONFIRMED — TXT-001]
**Last main meal: at least 3 hours before sleep onset (= 2 complete cycles).** Last light snack: no later than 90 minutes before sleep onset. Irregular meal times disrupt circadian rhythms. Breakfast regularity is particularly important for anchoring the circadian clock.

**P-079** [CONFIRMED — TXT-001]
**Caffeine ceiling: 400 mg/day** (EFSA recommendation). Half-life up to 6 hours — caffeine taken in mid-afternoon remains active at sleep onset. Reserve caffeine for moments requiring peak alertness (key meetings, critical tasks); do not use habitually for baseline functioning. PMers using caffeine to compensate for morning sluggishness get better long-term results from daylight exposure.

**P-080** [CONFIRMED — TXT-001]
**CRP espresso technique:** taking a single espresso (~75 mg caffeine) immediately before a 30-min CRP produces peak alertness at the end of the CRP — because caffeine takes ~20 minutes to activate. Use an espresso (rapid absorption, small volume) not a milky coffee. Provides both the CRP recovery benefit and a natural energy peak on waking from it.

**P-081** [CONFIRMED — TXT-001]
**Tryptophan-rich foods support melatonin production.** The body uses tryptophan to produce serotonin, which converts to melatonin. Include regularly: chicken, turkey, dairy, fish, bananas, nuts. **Montmorency (tart) cherries** are the single most evidence-backed food intervention: research by Prof. Glyn Howatson (Northumbria University) shows they increase melatonin production and improve sleep duration and quality. Available dried or as juice.

**P-082** [CONFIRMED — TXT-001]
**Alcohol is not prohibited.** A moderate amount during a social evening is acceptable. Excessive alcohol disrupts sleep continuity. Avoid alcohol on long-haul flights (dehydration amplifies the effect). The concern is excess, not the drink itself.

## Bedroom Temperature — Exact Range (from TXT-001)
> *Source: `Data Brut/Art de mieux dormir, L_ - Nick Littlehales.txt` · Chapter 7 (TXT-001)*

**P-083** [CONFIRMED — TXT-001]
**Optimal bedroom temperature: 16–18°C (61–64°F).** This is the precise range. The bedroom must be cooler than the rest of the house. Techniques to achieve: open window an hour before bed; turn off only the bedroom radiator; in summer — close curtains all day and use a fan; use ice water in front of a fan. ## Mattress Profiling — Precise Measurement (from TXT-001)
> *Source: `Data Brut/Art de mieux dormir, L_ - Nick Littlehales.txt` · Chapter 6 (TXT-001)*

**P-084** [CONFIRMED — TXT-001]
**Mattress gap test — exact threshold: 6 cm (≈ two flat hands).** Lie in foetal position on non-dominant side. If the gap between cheek/head and the mattress surface is ≥ 6 cm, the mattress is too firm. If the hip sinks and the spine is no longer horizontal = too soft. The correct mattress keeps the spine straight with little or no pillow required. A mattress frame is purely decorative — 100% of sleep kit budget should go to the sleeping surface.

**P-085** [CONFIRMED — TXT-001]
**Morphotype (body type) determines mattress surface profile.** Three types: (1) **Ectomorph** — slim, narrow hips, long limbs; needs least surface cushioning. (2) **Mesomorph** — intermediate, broad chest and shoulders; mid-range cushioning. (3) **Endomorph** — larger frame, broad shoulders and hips; needs most cushioning. For couples: the dominant morphotype (endomorph > mesomorph > ectomorph) determines the surface profile. The hands-gap test (P-084) is simpler and takes priority.

**P-086** [CONFIRMED — TXT-001]
**Sleep kit construction — layered, modular, replaceable.** Build the sleeping surface in layers: 2–3 layers of viscoelastic foam toppers (each 5–8 cm thick), covered by a topper cover. Add a thin éredon (comforter) on top — acts as a full-body pillow for limbs. One thin pillow compensates only for any remaining gap. Budget approach: spend €150–300 every 2 years on a new top layer rather than €1,500 every 10 years on a full mattress. Mattress on the floor (no frame) is entirely valid — lower, cooler, and used by many athletes.

## Teen and Child Sleep (from TXT-001)
> *Source: `Data Brut/Art de mieux dormir, L_ - Nick Littlehales.txt` · Chapter 10 (TXT-001)*

**P-087** [CONFIRMED — TXT-001]
**Teen athletes (mid-adolescence+): 6 cycles as the target** (not 5). ## Sleep Science — Dual Driver Model (from DOC-010)
> *Source: `Data Brut/IP Docs/R90 Methodology articles /Sleep Noise Blog Jan 2025.docx` · SLEEP book Chapter 1 excerpt (DOC-010)*

**P-149** [CONFIRMED — DOC-010]
**Sleep is governed by two separate but interacting biological systems: the circadian urge and homeostatic sleep pressure.** The circadian rhythm provides the biological clock — a 24-hour cycle telling the body *when* to sleep. Homeostatic sleep pressure is the accumulating *need* to sleep that builds from the moment of waking — the longer you are awake, the greater the pressure. These two systems are normally aligned (both peak at night), but the circadian rhythm can override homeostatic pressure — which is why people feel a "second wind" at night, and why shift workers struggle to sleep during daylight hours even after 24+ hours awake. The R90 framework works with both: ARP fixes the circadian anchor; CRPs and MRMs partially discharge homeostatic pressure during the day.

**Typical indoor work environments deliver only 200–300 lux — far below the daytime 1,000 lux target and even below the 500 lux melatonin suppression threshold.** Office, train, and gym environments typically operate at this level. Consequence: insufficient daytime light exposure causes premature melatonin production, triggering afternoon drowsiness and energy loss independent of sleep debt. Outdoor lux = 60,000–70,000 (consistent with PDF-001's 80,000 upper bound). Counter-tactic: get outside first thing and at intervals, or use a daylight lamp/circadian lighting system.

**P-144** [CONFIRMED — DOC-004]
**Pre-travel jet lag preparation starts weeks before departure, not at the airport.** Elite athlete protocol: gradually shift wake and sleep times toward destination time zone in the weeks leading up to travel, combined with appropriate light management — bright blue light in the destination's morning, absolute darkness at night. This pre-adaptation reduces the circadian disruption on arrival.

**P-145** [CONFIRMED — DOC-004]
**Post-arrival rule: immediately target sleep time aligned with the destination time zone, not the departure zone.** "Aim for a sleep time that is as close as possible to what your regular rhythm would be if you had always lived in that time zone." No gradual drift — commit to destination ARP from night one.

**P-146** [CONFIRMED — DOC-004]
**Late-arrival jet lag protocol (body clock says morning, local time says night):** (1) Avoid sleeping on the plane. (2) On arrival, immediately dim all lights. (3) Refrain from alcohol and heavy meals. (4) Use relaxing sensory activities — warm bath, music — to stimulate melatonin despite the body clock signal. (5) Ensure hotel room is completely dark and cool. Outcome: even 3 cycles is sufficient for the first night and will restore near-normal rhythm by the next day.

**P-147** [CONFIRMED — DOC-004]
**Early-arrival jet lag protocol (body clock says night, local time says morning):** (1) Sleep on the plane — use earplugs, eye mask, book window seat. (2) Request not to be woken. (3) After arrival, maximise natural daylight exposure throughout the day. (4) Use CRPs as daytime rest pockets to accumulate recovery without disrupting the new rhythm.

**P-148** [CONFIRMED — DOC-004]
**Travel disruption minimum: 3 nocturnal cycles is sufficient for a jet lag arrival night.** This is an exception to the standard 4-cycle floor, specific to travel disruption context: "Even if you end up only getting three cycles that night, it will be sufficient to get you through the next day and back into an almost natural sleep cycle." The body's resilience in a circadian-correctly-anchored sleep environment is greater than the anxiety around low cycle count suggests.

## Sleep Stage Distribution Within the Night (from DOC-013)
> *Source: `transcripts/DOC-013_Why_What_and_When.txt` · full article (DOC-013)*

**P-168** [CONFIRMED — DOC-013]
**Sleep stages are distributed unevenly across the night: deep sleep (NREM Stage 3) dominates the FIRST 2 CYCLES; REM sleep dominates LATER cycles.** Nick states explicitly: "Deep sleep accounts for a higher proportion of our sleep in earlier cycles, as our body prioritises getting this as soon as it can, while REM sleep accounts for a higher proportion in later cycles." Practical implication: the first two sleep cycles (typically 23:00–02:00 for ARP 06:30) are the most physiologically restorative. Missing or disrupting the early cycles causes the greatest loss of deep sleep; missing or disrupting the later cycles causes greater REM loss. Both matter, but the body prioritises deep sleep first.

**P-169** [CONFIRMED — DOC-013]
**When sleep-deprived, the brain compensates by entering REM sleep earlier in the cycle sequence.** "If we have been getting less sleep than normal, our brain will drop into REM for longer in earlier cycles, demonstrating its importance to us." This is an automatic biological recovery mechanism — the brain prioritises the most-needed stage rather than following the standard sequence. The body is "remarkably good" at targeting its specific deficits. Application: a shortened sleep night following sleep debt will naturally compress toward the stage most deficient — this is self-regulating, not requiring a manual intervention beyond providing the sleep opportunity.

**P-170** [CONFIRMED — DOC-013]
**Deep restorative stages are concentrated in cycles that begin between 23:00 and 02:00 (for standard ARP 06:30); after 02:00, deep sleep proportion decreases significantly.** DOC-013 bullet notation: "Deep Restorative stages first 2 cycles — 11pm > 12:30 > 2am not so much into 3:30am." For ARP 06:30: cycle 1 starts at 23:00, cycle 2 at 00:30, cycle 3 at 02:00, cycle 4 at 03:30. The first 2-3 cycles capture the majority of NREM Stage 3 deep sleep. This is the physiological basis for Nick's emphasis on early sleep onset — delaying the sleep window shifts the deep sleep window further from its optimal biological expression. The 10pm-02:00 deep-sleep premium window (from AUD-008) aligns with this principle.

## Light Exposure — Full Lux Hierarchy (from PPT-003)
> *Source: `transcripts/PPT-003_Cody_Ruberto_coaching_deck.txt` · Slides 8, 11 (PPT-003)*

**P-153** [CONFIRMED — PPT-003]
**Complete lux reference hierarchy for the R90 light management protocol:**
- Outside (full sun): up to 100,000 lux (seasonal); 60,000 lux typical; human average daytime exposure ≈ 10,000 lux
- DWS / dawn simulator device: 10,000 lux — mimics average human outdoor exposure at ARP
- By a window (indoors): ~3,000 lux
- Waking cycle average target (C2–C10): ~1,000 lux — the practical indoor light goal for active hours
- **500 lux = melatonin threshold** ("diminished light"): at or below 500 lux, melatonin production begins and functionality suppresses. Most interior rooms operate near or below this threshold.
- Typical office/workplace: 200–300 lux (well below the 500 lux threshold — see P-143)
Implication: most people spend their waking hours in melatonin-suppressing light conditions. Outdoor exposure at first light (C1) is the most critical correction.
