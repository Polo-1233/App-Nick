## Sleep Scheduling Rules (from DOC-002)
> *Source: `transcripts/02_sleep_in_cycles_final.txt` · lines 27–43*

**DR-011** [CONFIRMED — DOC-002]
Schedule sleep by counting **backwards from your target wake time in 90-minute increments**. This is the primary scheduling rule of R90. It ensures you wake at the end of a light stage, not mid-cycle.

**DR-012** [CONFIRMED — DOC-002]
Execute an **unhurried, sequenced post-sleep routine** immediately after waking: bladder → daylight → hydration → food → mental challenge → exercise → bowels. Do not skip or rush steps. The sequence matters.

**DR-013** [CONFIRMED — DOC-002]
Get **daylight exposure as soon as possible after waking** — before screens, before artificial light. This activates serotonin and resets the circadian clock for the day ahead.

**DR-014** [CONFIRMED — DOC-002]
Do not measure or worry about sleep quality as an outcome. Focus on process: consistent daily structure. Sleep anxiety is itself a primary cause of poor sleep.

## Environmental Protocol Rules (from AUD-004)
> *Source: `transcripts/Lesson_4.txt` · lines 22–187 (post-sleep sequence, polarity pairs, temperature, light protocol)*

**DR-027** [CONFIRMED — AUD-004]
**Morning: activate with light and temperature.** Within the first 90-minute block after ARP: move from dark to light (trigger serotonin), move from warm to slightly cool environment. This is the non-negotiable foundation of the post-sleep routine.

**DR-028** [CONFIRMED — AUD-004]
**Evening: shift to warm-spectrum light.** As the evening progresses — especially in the hour(s) before sleep — shift to amber, yellow, or red light. Eliminate or minimise blue/white light. This allows melatonin to build without forcing sleep.

**DR-029** [CONFIRMED — AUD-004]
**Bedroom: keep room a couple of degrees cooler than your body.** Use a pre-bed shower, light yoga, or brief exercise to raise body temperature slightly. When you enter the cooler room, the differential triggers sleep onset. Avoid the hot-room/heavy-blanket trap.

**DR-030** [CONFIRMED — AUD-004]
**Use the pre-sleep period to download the day.** Resolve unfinished thoughts, avoid starting new demanding work, allow the mental load to settle before the sleep window. The day's MRM and CRP structure enables this — without them, residue carries into the cycles.

## Structural Framework Rules (from DOC-003)
> *Source: `transcripts/03_sleep_recovery_final.txt` · lines 5, 15, 27, 29*

**DR-023** [CONFIRMED — DOC-003]
**Set your ARP (Anchor Reset Point) first.** This fixed wake time is the non-negotiable foundation. Every other scheduling decision — sleep onset, CRP, MRM — flows from it.

**DR-024** [CONFIRMED — DOC-003]
**Plan the full 16 cycles of the day**, not just the sleep portion. Alternate high-demand phases with recovery phases across all 16 blocks. The day is an integrated 24h recovery system, not "activity + sleep."

**DR-025** [CONFIRMED — DOC-003]
**Take MRMs (Micro Recovery Moments):** minimum 5-minute mental break every 90-minute cycle during waking hours. This is the floor, not the ceiling. Even this minimum measurably improves sleep onset ease and deep sleep access.

**DR-026** [CONFIRMED — DOC-003]
**Do not apply sleep interventions in isolation.** Mattress, tracker, app, supplement, or technique must sit within the structural SWR framework. Without the framework, isolated tools are at best ineffective and at worst counterproductive.

## Cycle-Based Scheduling Rules (from AUD-003)
> *Source: `transcripts/Lesson_3.txt` · lines 18–80 (cycles, weekly target, CRP window, fixed wake time, micro-break rhythm)*

**DR-018** [CONFIRMED — AUD-003]
**Fix your wake time. Never change it.** Flex sleep onset to get more or fewer cycles. The wake time is the master anchor — consistent 7 days a week.

**DR-019** [CONFIRMED — AUD-003]
Count in **complete 90-minute cycles**, not total hours. A 4.5h night (3 complete cycles) is better than a 5h night with an incomplete 4th cycle. Completeness matters more than duration.

**DR-020** [CONFIRMED — AUD-003]
**Manage recovery across the week, not nightly.** Track toward 35 cycles/week. A short night is not a failure — it is an input to the weekly balance, corrected with CRP.

**DR-021** [CONFIRMED — AUD-003, PPT-001]
When a short night is anticipated or has occurred, **schedule a CRP in Phase 2 (cycles 6/7/8, midday to early evening)** — 30 minutes. This adds 1 cycle and removes bedtime pressure. *(Earlier sources said "20–30 minutes" — normalised to 30 min per PPT-001.)*

**DR-022** [CONFIRMED — AUD-003]
**Every 90 minutes during the day:** take a brief micro-break. This is not optional or aspirational — it is the baseline polyphasic rhythm applied to waking hours.

## Sleep Product Rules (from AUD-007, DOC-006)
> *Source: `transcripts/Lesson_7.txt` (AUD-007) · lines 28–186; `transcripts/R90-T_Playbook_Overview_.txt` (DOC-006) · lines 790–820*

**DR-031** [CONFIRMED — AUD-007, DOC-006]
**Sleep in the foetal position on your non-dominant side.** Right-handers: left side. Left-handers: right side. This is the default coaching position — biomechanically less stressful and triggers the brain's security/sleep-onset signal. Do not force it, but set it as the target.

**DR-032** [CONFIRMED — AUD-007]
**Conduct a mattress check before buying products.** Lie on the floor in foetal position on your non-dominant side. Then repeat on your mattress. If the cheek-to-surface gap is greater than a hand's width, the mattress is too firm. Remedy: add a soft layer before purchasing a new mattress.

**DR-033** [CONFIRMED — AUD-007]
**Use individual duvets for sleeping partners with different temperature preferences.** Do not force a shared duvet on different body types and thermal needs. Individual duvets eliminate a common unacknowledged sleep disruptor.

**DR-034** [CONFIRMED — AUD-007]
**Increase bed linen change frequency.** The fresh-linen familiarisation effect (cooler, cleaner = positive sleep signal) degrades by night 2. If sleep quality is an issue, increase change frequency as a first, zero-cost intervention.

**DR-035** [CONFIRMED — AUD-007]
**Train nose breathing during sleep.** Mouth breathing is common but disruptive. If it is identified as a factor, use available training tools and techniques to restore natural nasal breathing — this directly improves cycle access and airway comfort.

## Intra-Cycle and Schedule Recovery Rules (from DOC-006)
> *Source: `transcripts/R90-T_Playbook_Overview_.txt` · lines 466–508, 680, 854*

**DR-036** [CONFIRMED — DOC-006]
**If you come home late and can't hit your normal sleep onset: wait for the next cycle, don't force it.** Missing one cycle by 15–30 minutes and waiting for the next boundary produces better sleep than trying to sleep mid-cycle. "It's a bit like missing a bus — just catch the next one."

**DR-037** [CONFIRMED — DOC-006]
**If sleep hasn't come within 15 minutes: get up.** Remain in chillout mode (no screens, no demanding tasks), wait for the next 90-minute cycle boundary, then try again. Do not lie anxious in bed — this compounds the problem rather than resolving it.

**DR-038** [CONFIRMED — DOC-006]
**Target 10,000 Lux in the first cycle (post-ARP).** Get outside ideally, or use a 10,000 Lux light therapy device. This triggers the serotonin and cortisol spike that resets the inner clock and determines when melatonin will rise ~12 hours later. It is the master lever of the post-sleep routine.

> *Source: `transcripts/Lesson_6.txt` · lines 44–245*

**DR-039** [CONFIRMED — AUD-006]
**Always pair blackout blinds with a Dawn Wake Simulator.** Blackout without a DWS removes the serotonin trigger. A DWS without blackout is redundant. They operate as a unit — install both or reconsider the approach.

**DR-040** [CONFIRMED — AUD-006]
**When sleeping in an unfamiliar environment, bring personal familiarisation tools.** Identify your key sensory cues (smell, comfort object, sound, visual) and take at least one with you. Do the hotel room mental strip — identify what can be moved, dimmed, or covered. A familiar sensory anchor partially compensates for the unfamiliar environment.

**DR-041** [CONFIRMED — AUD-006]
**Do the mental strip exercise before purchasing any bedroom product.** Empty the room mentally, then only return things that serve recovery. Anything that remains purely for stimulation (TV, work items, harsh lighting) is a barrier to deep sleep access — address before spending on new products.

## Sleep Disorder Prevention Rules (from AUD-008)
> *Source: `transcripts/Lesson_8.txt` · lines 49–244*

**DR-042** [CONFIRMED — AUD-008]
**On waking at 2–3am: do not panic, do not force sleep.** Get up, stay in chillout mode (no overstimulating light or activity), wait for the next 90-minute cycle boundary. Sleep onset will return. Treating 2–3am wakefulness as a crisis compounds it into genuine insomnia.

**DR-043** [CONFIRMED — AUD-008]
**When sleep has been disrupted, use CRP to rebalance — do not go to bed earlier or sleep in later.** "Just start your day well with that post-sleep period, knowing that later on in the day you may be able to grab a shorter period, that little 30-minute slot, that little CRP, and we can put that in to balance the approach." Disrupted nights are managed at the weekly cycle level, not by shifting the ARP.

**DR-044** [CONFIRMED — AUD-008]
**Treat sleep tracker data as a guide, not a verdict.** If tracker data increases anxiety, the tracker is now a sleep disruptor (ortho-insomnia risk). Calibrate use: check data contextually against how you feel, not as a primary performance score.

**DR-045** [CONFIRMED — AUD-008]
**For persistent disrupted sleep, use sleep restriction to reset.** Choose a later cycle entry time for a defined period (e.g., shift sleep onset from 23:00 to 00:30 or 02:00). Compensate with CRPs. The goal is to consolidate cycles and strengthen the circadian signal — not to accumulate hours.

## Nutrition, Meal Timing, and Substance Rules (from TXT-001)
> *Source: `Data Brut/Art de mieux dormir, L_ - Nick Littlehales.txt` (TXT-001) · Chapters 2, 4, 5, 8*

**DR-046** [CONFIRMED — TXT-001]
**Last main meal at least 3 hours (2 cycles) before sleep onset.** Last light snack no later than 90 minutes before sleep onset. "Is it too late to eat at 9pm?" — only relative to your bedtime. If sleep onset is 12:30am, a 9pm dinner is well-timed.

**DR-047** [CONFIRMED — TXT-001]
**Caffeine ceiling: 400 mg/day.** Caffeine half-life is up to 6 hours — taken at 15:00 remains active at 21:00 sleep onset. Strategic use only: reserve for moments of required peak alertness. Habitual use for baseline functioning leads to the stimulant compensation pattern (BP-022). **CRP espresso technique**: take a single espresso immediately before a 30-min CRP — caffeine activates in ~20 min, producing an energy peak at the end of the rest period.

**DR-048** [CONFIRMED — TXT-001]
**CRP evening window (5pm–7pm): maximum 30 minutes.** A full 90-minute cycle during the evening CRP window risks disrupting nocturnal sleep onset. The 30-minute cap applies strictly to the secondary window. The primary window (1pm–3pm) allows a full 90-minute CRP if the schedule permits — but plan for sleep inertia (extra 10–15 minutes) before any cognitive demand.

## Environmental Precision Rules (from TXT-001)
> *Source: `Data Brut/Art de mieux dormir, L_ - Nick Littlehales.txt` (TXT-001) · Chapters 6, 7*

**DR-050** [CONFIRMED — TXT-001]
**Bedroom temperature target: 16–18°C.** This is the explicit optimal range. Achieve it through: open window pre-sleep; bedroom radiator off; fan in summer; avoiding electric blankets (pre-warm only, then remove). A warm shower before bed (finishing warm, not hot) creates the body temperature elevation needed for the warm-to-cool transition into sleep.

**DR-051** [CONFIRMED — TXT-001]
**Mattress gap threshold: ≥ 6 cm = too firm.** Perform the floor test (lie in non-dominant foetal); if the gap between cheek and mattress surface is ≥ 6 cm (approximately two flat hands stacked), add a soft topper layer. Do not purchase a new mattress until this test is performed — a topper is almost always the correct and cheaper solution.

**DR-052** [CONFIRMED — TXT-001]
**Switch all electronic devices to full off — not standby — before sleep.** Standby LEDs emit light that reaches the pineal gland and suppresses melatonin. Bathroom bright lighting is equally disruptive: complete teeth-brushing and final hygiene before the amber light transition window, or switch to a soft bulb or candlelight.

## Shift Work and Jet Lag Rules (from TXT-001)
> *Source: `Data Brut/Art de mieux dormir, L_ - Nick Littlehales.txt` (TXT-001) · Chapter 9*

**DR-053** [CONFIRMED — TXT-001]
**Shift workers: fix an evening ARP (e.g., 6:30 pm) just as day workers fix a morning ARP.** After a night shift, do not go immediately to bed — eat first, decompress. Target sleep onset approximately 12:30 pm to align with the circadian trough equivalent. Use blackout provision at all times. During the night shift, use daylight lamps to suppress melatonin and maintain alertness. Schedule CRP at 2–3 am (natural circadian trough during night shifts).

**DR-054** [CONFIRMED — TXT-001]
**Jet lag: manage with light, not medication.** For eastward travel (harder): begin advancing biological clock before departure — earlier wake, earlier light exposure, earlier bedtime with darkened environment. For westward (easier): delay the clock. In-flight: manage window light and light exposure to match destination time. Post-arrival: immediately adopt destination light schedule. Priority: light management > caffeine > sleeping pills. Sleeping pills are not recommended for jet lag management.

## Sleep Disruption Rules (from DOC-007)
> *Source: `Data Brut/IP Docs/R90 Playbook/28-08 PB clean content.docx` · KSPI 4, KSPI 6 (DOC-007)*

**DR-058** [CONFIRMED — DOC-007]
**15-minute rule: IF you cannot enter sleep within 15 minutes THEN stop trying.** Get up. Stay in a low-stimulation chillout phase (200 lux or less, no tech). Wait for the next 90-minute cycle boundary. Return to bed at that point. Do not keep lying in bed trying to force onset.

**DR-059** [CONFIRMED — DOC-007]
**2–3am waking rule: IF you wake during the third cycle (02:00–03:00) and cannot return to sleep THEN get up, chillout, wait for the next cycle.** Do not check the time repeatedly. Do not catastrophise. The wait may be a full cycle (up to 90 minutes). This converts a panic-state into a structured protocol. [Reinforces BP-031]

**DR-060** [CONFIRMED — DOC-007]
**"Miss the bus" rule: IF you miss your planned sleep cycle start time THEN wait for the next cycle — do not go to bed between cycle boundaries.** Going to bed mid-cycle means entering sleep at the wrong phase. Accept the later cycle; accept a four-cycle night. The ARP the following morning is unchanged. Never shift ARP to compensate for a short night.

**DR-061** [CONFIRMED — DOC-007]
**Earlier-than-planned sleep: IF an earlier start becomes available or needed THEN move back one cycle.** Go to bed one 90-minute cycle earlier than originally planned. Do not go to bed at an arbitrary non-cycle time.

## Life Event Adaptation Rules (from AUD-009)

**DR-066** [CONFIRMED — AUD-009]
**Recovery day rule: IF a controlled recovery day is available THEN (1) wake at ARP, (2) complete Cycle 1 post-sleep routine in chilled (low-activation) mode, (3) return to bed for 1–2 additional 90-min cycles with alarm set; OR (4) schedule 2 × 90-min recovery cycles in the midday window (12:00–16:00).** Never drift past ARP without setting an alarm. **DR-067** [CONFIRMED — AUD-009]

**Illness mode rule: IF the user is ill THEN shift recovery emphasis to daytime polyphasic cycles; maximise midday and early-evening CRP slots; take pressure off nocturnal performance; optimise environment (airflow, cleanliness, nasal breathing); do not attempt to maintain full nocturnal cycle targets during acute illness.**

**DR-069** [CONFIRMED — AUD-009]

## Applied Coaching Case Rules (from PPT-004)
> *Source: `Data Brut/Workshop Coaching Decks/Coaching Case study decks/MA-Elite R90-T 7.0 Playbook pptx.pptx` (PPT-004)*
> *Derived from Nick's live coaching session for Majid Alibrahim (PMer, ARP 07:00).*

**DR-072** [CONFIRMED — PPT-004]
**PMer 4-cycle strategic target: IF chronotype = PMer AND occupational ARP forces an early wake time AND 5-cycle onset (ARP − 7.5h) is chronically unachievable THEN reframe 4 undisturbed cycles (onset at ARP − 6h) as the primary operational target.** Do not treat 4 cycles as a deficit floor — present it as the PMer's optimised plan: "4 cycles & 6 hours undisturbed + your MRMs & CRP could be a game changer." Nick's framing: a clean 4-cycle block from 01:00 to 07:00 is superior to a disrupted 5-cycle attempt starting at 23:30 when the PMer is not biologically ready for sleep.

**DR-074** [CONFIRMED — DOC-004]
**Jet lag travel rule: at destination, immediately adopt local time zone ARP — no gradual drift.** Do not try to "meet in the middle." Commit to the destination sleep time from night one, using light management and pre-sleep tactics to support onset despite the body clock mismatch.

**DR-075** [CONFIRMED — DOC-004]
**Travel minimum floor: 3 nocturnal cycles is acceptable for a jet lag disruption night.** This is a travel-specific exception to the standard 4-cycle floor. Three cycles is sufficient to restore near-normal rhythm by the following day when combined with correct light exposure and a commitment to destination ARP.

**DR-076** [CONFIRMED — DOC-004]
**Layover strategy: use transit time as a deliberate CRP or rest cycle.** Target flight combinations that allow a quiet rest opportunity in the airport. Do not allow yourself to become fully exhausted during travel — arrive with at least one rest cycle already taken.

**DR-073** [CONFIRMED — PDF-001]
**7-Day Reset Target (active recovery mode) = 28 nocturnal cycles + 42 MRMs + 7 CRPs.** Distinct from the "no-worries reality actuals" floor (28 cycles + 35 MRMs + 5 CRPs). When in active recovery mode after a disrupted period, the target is to maintain FULL CRP and MRM practice (42 and 7) while accepting the lower nocturnal cycle count of 28. The "no-worries" floor describes what most people realistically achieve; the "reset target" describes what someone actively using the framework should aim for during recovery. Source: `PDF-001`, page 147 template header + page 95 narrative ("28 back to back 90-minute sleeping cycles combined with 7 CRPs and 42 MRMs, will help you maintain a rhythm and balance").

## Shift Work Rules (from IMG-016, IMG-017)

**DR-078** [CONFIRMED — IMG-016, IMG-017]
**Shift work ARP selection rule: determine ARP 1 or ARP 2 before any scheduling.** IF client works day shifts (active during daylight hours) → ARP 1 = 06:00. IF client works night shifts (active during overnight hours) → ARP 2 = 18:00. All 16 cycle start times, phase boundaries, sleep window, and CRP placement are then calculated using DR-077 (Cn = ARP + (n−1) × 90 min) from the applicable ARP. Night shift (ARP 2 = 18:00): CRP at C7 = 03:00, at the midpoint of the active overnight period. General rule: CRP should be placed approximately 8–9 cycles after ARP (C8/C9 area), which falls naturally at the midpoint of the active waking period regardless of ARP.

## 16-Cycle Scheduling Rules (from IMG-010)
> *Source: `Data Brut/IP Docs/R90 Playbook/PB Illustrator graphics/R90 Infographs/Everyday Infographs/7 day forecast 06.30 ARP Infograph .png` (IMG-010)*

**DR-077** [CONFIRMED — IMG-010]
**To find any cycle's start time: Cn = ARP + (n − 1) × 90 minutes.** For ARP 06:30: C1=06:30, C2=08:00, C3=09:30 … C5=12:30 (Phase 2 start), C9=18:30 (Phase 3 start), C12=23:00 (5-cycle bedtime), C13=00:30 (4-cycle bedtime / Phase 4 start), C16=05:00. This formula applies universally — substitute any ARP to generate a personalised cycle table. Phase boundaries always fall at C1, C5, C9, C13 (i.e., every 4 cycles).
