# R90 Behavioural Patterns

Patterns in how people relate to sleep — both problematic and desirable.
Nick uses these to diagnose client situations and design interventions.

---

## Problematic Patterns (What R90 Corrects)

**BP-001 — Normalised Underperformance** [CONFIRMED — DOC-001]
People continue functioning under chronic sleep deprivation without recognising the deficit. They "crack on" without acknowledging they are a shadow of their optimal self. The degradation becomes the new baseline.

**BP-002 — Sleep as Weakness or Waste** [CONFIRMED — DOC-001]
A common cultural belief that sleeping less is productive, that sleep is "something to do when you have passed away." Nick explicitly names and rejects this mindset.

**BP-003 — Addictive Counterproductive Behaviour** [CONFIRMED — DOC-001]
Poor sleep leads to impulsive choices and unhealthy habits that become self-reinforcing. The behaviour loop compounds the sleep problem rather than solving it.

**BP-004 — Working Against the Circadian Rhythm** [STRONG INFERENCE — IMG-001]
Ignoring natural biological timing (melatonin cycles, temperature curves, alertness peaks) leads to misaligned sleep and performance. Common in shift workers, frequent travellers, and high-demand professionals.

---

**BP-011** [CONFIRMED — DOC-002]
**Sleep as binary:** Treating sleep as on/off ("I slept / I didn't sleep") ignores stages and cycles. This misconception causes people to optimise the wrong things (total hours) rather than cycle completion and timing.

**BP-012** [CONFIRMED — DOC-002]
**Rushed post-sleep:** Alarm → immediate activity, no daylight, no hydration. This skips the circadian reset and degrades the entire day. Extremely common. Directly addressed by the post-sleep routine.

**BP-013** [CONFIRMED — DOC-002]
**Sleep anxiety loop:** Worrying about poor sleep → worse sleep → more worry. The biggest single disruptor of sleep. R90 breaks this by shifting focus from outcome to process.

## Desirable Patterns (What R90 Builds)
> *BP-005–BP-007: `transcripts/01_sleep_and_performance_final.txt` · lines 3–59 (DOC-001)*
> *BP-006 also draws on `transcripts/Circadian_Rhythm_Infograph.txt` (IMG-001, OCR — approximate)*
> *BP-008–BP-010: `transcripts/Lesson_1.txt` · lines 21–64 (AUD-001)*

**BP-005 — Structured Daily Intervention** [CONFIRMED — DOC-001]
R90 clients adopt a set of intentional behaviours distributed across the day — not just at bedtime — to support both quality and quantity of sleep.

**BP-006 — Working With Biology** [STRONG INFERENCE — IMG-001]
Aligning activity, rest, and sleep to the circadian rhythm rather than fighting it. Scheduling performance in peak biological windows, protecting recovery windows.

**BP-007 — Positive Habit Formation** [CONFIRMED — DOC-001]
Good sleep promotes the discipline and clarity needed to build other healthy habits. Sleep is described as a foundation for, not a result of, broader health behaviour.

**BP-008** [CONFIRMED — AUD-001]
**Monophasic default:** Modern humans default to a single sleep block at night. Nick frames this explicitly as a recent aberration — not the natural human pattern. Polyphasic sleep was the norm before electric light.

**BP-009** [CONFIRMED — AUD-001]
**Eliminated micro-recovery:** The 24-7 culture removed natural passive recovery moments that existed when "there was nothing else to do." Technology filled these gaps. Consciously restoring them is part of R90.

**BP-010** [CONFIRMED — AUD-001]
**Circadian unawareness:** Most people have no practical knowledge of their circadian biology. Nick's first lesson simply asks clients to become aware of it — awareness precedes behavioural change.

**BP-020** [CONFIRMED — DOC-003]
**Electronic Insomnia:** A "can't switch it off" state driven by screens, gaming, and social media extending into the melatonin window. Nick classifies this as a distinct modern pathology — not ordinary insomnia — with a rapid rise in athletes over the past decade.

**BP-021** [CONFIRMED — DOC-003]
**Intervention scatter:** Responding to poor sleep with isolated products (mattress, tracker, app, supplement, meditation) without a structural plan. Each tool may have individual merit but without a framework they underperform and can compound the problem. This is among the most common and counterproductive patterns Nick observes.

**BP-022** [CONFIRMED — DOC-003]
**Stimulant escalation:** Progressive reliance on caffeine, energy drinks, herbal boosters, and supplements to compensate for poor recovery. Consistent with the "camouflaging" pattern from AUD-002 — masking the symptom while deepening the structural problem.

---

**BP-014** [CONFIRMED — AUD-002]
**Chronotype suppression:** PMers (70% of the population) are forced to operate on AMer schedules. They compensate with caffeine and stimulants rather than acknowledging their biology. Nick calls this "camouflaging" — it works short-term and causes cumulative harm.

**BP-015** [CONFIRMED — AUD-002]
**Misaligned exercise:** People exercise when socially convenient, not when their chronotype peaks. AMers training late disrupt sleep onset; PMers forced into early morning sessions underperform. Both reduce outcomes.

**BP-017** [CONFIRMED — AUD-003]
**Eight-hour fixation:** "I need 8 hours" is the most common sleep belief Nick encounters. It creates nightly anxiety, ignores cycle quality, and fails to account for daytime recovery. R90 replaces it with cycle counting and weekly targets.

**BP-018** [CONFIRMED — AUD-003]
**CRP stigma:** "Napping" is socially coded as weakness in professional and athletic contexts. This prevents people from using a legitimate recovery tool. Nick explicitly reframes it as a Controlled Recovery Period to dismantle the stigma.

**BP-019** [CONFIRMED — AUD-003]
**Evening pressure trap:** Without daytime recovery, people feel forced to go to bed early to accumulate hours. This creates bedtime anxiety and often backfires into poor sleep onset. CRP relieves the pressure by adding cycles earlier in the day.

**BP-023** [CONFIRMED — AUD-004]
**Overheated bedroom:** Most people prefer a warm, heavy-bedding environment. This prevents the body-to-room temperature differential that signals sleep onset — one of the most common and quietly damaging environmental mistakes.

**BP-024** [CONFIRMED — AUD-004]
**Bright light in the evening:** Continued use of full white/blue light (screens, overhead lighting) through the evening suppresses melatonin and actively delays sleep onset. People are unaware they are fighting their own biology in real time.

**BP-025** [CONFIRMED — AUD-004]
**Day-long neglect, last-minute fix:** The assumption that a 30-minute pre-sleep routine can undo 16 hours of poor conditions. Nick directly challenges this as illogical. The entire day is the preparation — no shortcut exists.

---

**BP-026** [CONFIRMED — AUD-007]
**Pillow dependency.** The reflex to use multiple or specialist pillows (ergonomic, anti-snoring, physio) is typically a symptom of a too-firm mattress surface — not a standalone problem. Addressing the pillow symptom in isolation while ignoring mattress surface does not resolve the underlying issue.

**BP-027** [CONFIRMED — AUD-007]
**Mouth breathing default.** Many people habitually breathe through the mouth during sleep, often without awareness. This blocks natural airway function, contributes to snoring and dry mouth, and reduces sleep quality. Trainable — but rarely addressed as a sleep intervention.

**BP-028** [CONFIRMED — DOC-006]
**Sleep Noise / Device Dipping loop.** Under sleep pressure, people search online for sleep solutions. This triggers sleep-anxiety-content algorithms, which serve more alarming sleep content, which deepens anxiety, which worsens sleep. The cycle is self-reinforcing. Nick explicitly names this as a modern pathology and positions R90 as an exit from the loop.

**BP-029** [CONFIRMED — DOC-006]
**Social Jet Lag acceptance.** PMers normalise chronotype misalignment — they accept early starts, stimulant reliance, and persistent under-performance as fixed features of their life rather than recognising them as symptoms of a schedule that conflicts with their biology.

**BP-030** [CONFIRMED — AUD-008]
**Ortho-insomnia spiral.** Using a sleep tracker to improve sleep, then developing anxiety about the data, which worsens sleep, which produces worse data, which deepens anxiety. A self-reinforcing loop created entirely by the measurement tool. Common in high-achievers who apply a performance-metrics mindset to sleep.

**BP-031** [CONFIRMED — AUD-008]
**2–3am panic.** Waking at 2–3am and immediately catastrophising ("I've only had X hours, I'm going to be wrecked"). The panic response raises serotonin, prevents re-entry into the next cycle, and turns a natural polyphasic transition into genuine sleep disruption. R90's response: chill, wait, the cycle will return.

**BP-032** [CONFIRMED — AUD-006]
**Overstimulated bedroom.** Accumulation of televisions, work items, devices, harsh lighting, and stimulating décor in the sleeping space — each individually justified, collectively destroying the brain's association of the room with recovery. The most common environmental barrier to deep sleep access.

## Additional Patterns (from TXT-001)
> *Source: `Data Brut/Art de mieux dormir, L_ - Nick Littlehales.txt` · Chapters 2, 4, 7, 9, 10 (TXT-001)*

**BP-033** [CONFIRMED — TXT-001]
**Alcohol-as-sleep-aid.** Using alcohol to fall asleep. Produces a pleasant subjective feeling of sleepiness but disrupts sleep continuity and reduces cycle quality. Common in adults under stress or with onset anxiety. Not addressed by quantity limits alone — the underlying anxiety or pattern driving the habit must be resolved.

**BP-034** [CONFIRMED — TXT-001]
**Sleep medication dependency.** Relying on prescribed or over-the-counter sleep medications beyond situational use. Z-drugs improve sleep onset by only ~22 minutes vs. placebo at significant risk cost. Nick's protocol: stop immediately; replace with structural R90 framework. The medication addresses the symptom — the structural deficit is the cause.

**BP-035** [CONFIRMED — TXT-001]
**Overlooked light at the critical pre-sleep window.** Completing the hygiene routine under bright bathroom fluorescent lighting, or leaving TV/device standby LEDs active during sleep. Both are unrecognised melatonin suppressors acting at the most sensitive point in the pre-sleep window. Bathroom issue: switch to a soft warm bulb or complete hygiene earlier. Standby issue: full off, not standby.

**BP-036** [CONFIRMED — TXT-001]
**Ignoring the couple's sleep system.** Sharing a double bed (140 cm) with a single duvet without accounting for dominant hand, sleeping side, temperature needs, or morphotype compatibility. Sleep disturbance from a partner is the second most common cause of poor sleep in adults (after stress/worry). Solution: individual duvets, appropriate bed size, bed-side selection by dominant hand.

**BP-037** [CONFIRMED — TXT-001]
**Teen chronotype denial.** Treating an adolescent's naturally later sleep timing as laziness rather than a puberty-driven biological clock shift. Forces teens into early school schedules that conflict with their melatonin production. Nick advocates school start times no earlier than 10 am.

**BP-038** [CONFIRMED — TXT-001]
**Training at convenience, not at the circadian peak.** Exercising at social convenience (early morning because it fits the diary) rather than at the chronotype-appropriate peak performance window. Most athletic world records are set in the afternoon and evening — the circadian peak for physical performance. Misaligned training reduces both performance and recovery.

**BP-039** [CONFIRMED — AUD-010]
**Bedroom deprioritisation in property decisions.** People buying or renting a home evaluate kitchens, lounges, balconies, and gardens first — the bedroom is rarely assessed as a primary criterion. As a result, they end up in bedrooms that are too small for a super-king mattress, have no blackout provision, cannot be adequately cooled, or cannot accommodate two people with different sleep profiles. Nick explicitly names this as a missed performance opportunity: "we actually should focus more on: is the bedroom big enough?"

**BP-040** [CONFIRMED — AUD-009]
**Duvet day drift.** When intending to "catch up" on sleep, people abandon their ARP entirely — staying in bed until 10am, 11am, or beyond, without a plan or alarm. This uncontrolled drift disrupts the circadian rhythm, produces groggy, non-restorative extended sleep, and makes the following night harder. Nick explicitly contrasts this with the structured recovery day: wake at ARP, complete Cycle 1, then return to bed with an alarm for 1–2 controlled cycles.

**BP-041** [CONFIRMED — DOC-007]
**Sleep passivity ("sleep dating" baseline).** The majority of people do almost nothing proactive about their sleep — they simply react to it. When asked five diagnostic questions about what they actively do for sleep, most people answer "nothing" or "not consistently." The baseline expectation is minimal: just wanting sleep to "not feel like a waste of time." R90 works with this baseline — it does not demand a complete overhaul, it introduces one proactive habit at a time.

**BP-042** [CONFIRMED — DOC-007]
**Mid-night panic and clock-checking.** On waking at 2–3am, the immediate reflex is to check the time and calculate remaining hours ("I've only got 4 hours left"). This calculation triggers anxiety, raises cortisol, and actively prevents return to sleep. The 2–3am wake is normal; the catastrophising response is the problem. [Related to BP-031 — ortho-insomnia spiral but broader: applies even without a tracker]

**BP-043** [CONFIRMED — DOC-007]
**Compensatory lie-in after a short night.** Sleeping in on weekends or after a disrupted night to "make up" for lost sleep. This shifts the ARP, disrupts the circadian rhythm, and perpetuates the Monday-morning drag. Nick explicitly discourages lie-ins as a compensatory strategy — the CRP the following day is the correct tool.
