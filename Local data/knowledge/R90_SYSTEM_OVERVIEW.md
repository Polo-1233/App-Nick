## 1. Core Philosophy of Sleep and Recovery

### What R90 is

R90 is not a sleep hygiene protocol. It is a full restructuring of the 24-hour day around human biology. The central argument: sleep is not what happens at night — it is the output of how the entire preceding 24 hours was managed. Most sleep interventions fail because they address only the last 30 minutes before bed. R90 addresses all 16 × 90-minute cycles.

> "It does not focus on what happens when you are asleep, but on everything you do when awake." — DOC-006

### The three pillars [CONFIRMED — DOC-001, DOC-006]

Nick positions sleep as the **1st Health Pillar** — foundational to exercise and nutrition, which cannot deliver their full value without adequate recovery. The four active health pillars in his framing are: **sleepING, hydrating, eating, exercising** (note the deliberate -ING suffix — P-056).

Whether sleep is formally ranked *above* exercise and nutrition in a strict hierarchy, or whether "1st Pillar" is rhetorical emphasis, is **unresolved** (OQ-003).

### The target state [CONFIRMED — DOC-001]

**Personal Best (PB)** — the recoverable, consistent optimal performance level for each individual. R90 exists to make PB achievable more days than not. The word "recoverable" is deliberate: PB is not a ceiling, it is a repeatable floor. (P-003)

### The main disruptor [CONFIRMED — DOC-006, DOC-002, PPT-001]

**Worrying about sleep is sleep's primary disruptor.** This is stated multiple times across multiple sources as the lead principle. R90's structural response: shift focus from sleep outcomes (how many hours, how I feel) to sleep process (cycles completed, routines executed). Process focus eliminates the anxiety loop. (P-024, P-054, BP-013)

### The SleepING reframe [CONFIRMED — DOC-006, PPT-001]

Adding "ING" to sleep repositions it as an active daily behaviour — not a passive state you fall into. This mindset shift is Nick's first coaching intervention: **you are sleepING, just as you are hydrating, eating, exercising.** (P-056)

## 2. The 90-Minute Cycle Model

### The fundamental unit [CONFIRMED — DOC-002, AUD-003, DOC-006]

Sleep — and by extension the entire 24-hour day — is measured in **90-minute cycles**, not hours. This unit is grounded in clinical sleep measurement (polysomnography, hypnograms). Each cycle descends through four stages:

| Stage | Type | Function |
|-------|------|---------|
| 1 | NREM — Light | Transition from wake; easily disturbed |
| 2 | NREM — Deeper | Heart rate and body temp drop |
| 3 | NREM — Deep (SWS) | Physical repair: growth hormone, glycogen, protein synthesis |
| 4 | REM | Memory consolidation, creativity, emotional processing |

The **staircase metaphor** (P-048): top of stairs = light sleep, bottom = deep/REM. Only **20–25% of each cycle** is accessible as deep or REM sleep. Everything the person did during the preceding 24 hours — food, fluid, light, thoughts — determines how far down they travel.

### The 16-cycle day [CONFIRMED — DOC-002, DOC-006]

24 hours ÷ 90 minutes = **16 cycles exactly**. The R90 technique structures all 16 cycles, not just the nocturnal ones. The day is an integrated recovery system:

```
Phase 1 (cycles 1–4):   ARP → Midday        — activation, performance
Phase 2 (cycles 5–8):   Midday → Eve        — CRP window (cycles 6/7/8), continued activity
Phase 3 (cycles 8–12):  Eve → Sleep onset   — wind-down, pre-sleep preparation
Phase 4 (cycles 12–16): Nocturnal sleep     — 3–6 sleeping cycles
```

The four phases map to the circadian rhythm: Sunrise (P1) → Midday (P2) → Sunset (P3) → Night (P4). (P-053, OQ-024 resolved)

### Nocturnal targets [CONFIRMED — AUD-003, DOC-006]

| Target | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Daily nocturnal cycles | 5 (= 7.5h equivalent) |
| Weekly cycle target | 35 (5/day × 7 days) |
| Proportion of 24h in sleep | ~30% |
| Primary deep sleep window | 10pm–2am [CONFIRMED — AUD-008] |

The **"8 hours" myth** is explicitly debunked (P-034). 5 complete 90-minute cycles (7.5h) is the R90 equivalent, but cycle completion matters more than duration. A 3-cycle night is recoverable within the week (P-035).

### Cycle completeness [CONFIRMED — AUD-003]

Waking at the end of a cycle (top of the staircase = light sleep) produces alertness. Waking mid-cycle produces grogginess. This is the mechanical reason for counting backwards from the ARP when setting sleep onset times (DR-011). A 4.5h night (3 complete cycles) is explicitly better than a 5h night with an incomplete 4th cycle.

## 3. Chronotypes

### The two primary types [CONFIRMED — AUD-002, DOC-006]

Chronotype is **genetic and innate** — it cannot be changed, only worked with or suppressed. It determines the timing of the melatonin-to-serotonin shift in the pineal gland.

| | **AMer (Morning / Lark)** | **PMer (Evening / Owl)** |
|--|--------------------------|--------------------------|
| Natural wake | 5–7am | 8–9am or later |
| Peak performance | Morning (Phase 1 & 2) | Late afternoon / evening (Phase 3) |
| Pineal shift | Fast at sunrise | ~1–2 hour phase delay |
| Population | ~30% | ~70% |
| Risk zone | Late exercise, evening food/stimulants | Early-morning forced starts, stimulant reliance |

The majority (70%) live in a world structured for AMers. This chronotype mismatch is the root cause of widespread stimulant reliance and is what Nick calls **Social Jet Lag** (P-030, P-057).

### In-betweeners [CONFIRMED — AUD-002, DOC-006]

A third emerging pattern: people whose chronotype has been blurred by lifestyle, occupation, and social habits. They camouflage with caffeine and stimulants. Nick describes this as a **problematic pattern**, not a genuine third chronotype. (P-030, BP-014)

Whether "in-betweener" is formally defined in the R90 profiling system remains **partially uncertain** (OQ-021).

### Practical chronotype implications [CONFIRMED — AUD-002]

- Schedule key decisions and high-performance tasks in the person's natural peak window (DR-015)
- Do not force AMers into late exercise or PMers into early morning high-intensity performance without compensating with CRP (DR-016, DR-017)
- The circadian clock times from IMG-001 represent the **AMer baseline** — PMers shift all windows ~1–2 hours later (P-009, OQ-006 resolved)

## 4. Circadian Rhythm and Biological Timing

### The biological clock [CONFIRMED — AUD-001, DOC-006]

The circadian rhythm is a 24-hour biological cycle driven by:
- **Light/dark** — blue daylight triggers serotonin (activation); diminished amber light triggers melatonin (suppression)
- **Temperature** — body temperature cycles in parallel with the sleep/wake rhythm
- **Pineal gland** — produces melatonin and serotonin; the site of the chronotype-determining shift speed
- **Hypothalamus** — houses the internal body clock; syncs with external cues

Modern life disrupts this clock via three forces (P-015):
1. Electric light (~1700s) — extended wakefulness, suppressed natural cues
2. 24/7 technology — eliminated natural micro-recovery moments
3. Daylight saving time — artificial seasonal clock shift

### Key circadian markers [STRONG INFERENCE — IMG-001; approximate, OCR-derived]

> ⚠️ The following times are from OCR of a circular infographic (~60% accuracy). Treat as approximate until confirmed by Playbook sources.

| Time | Event |
|------|-------|
| ~06:00 | Lowest body temperature; serotonin begins; melatonin stops |
| ~08:30 | Sharpest blood pressure rise |
| ~10:00 | Highest alertness |
| ~12:00 | Fastest reaction time |
| ~14:30 | Best coordination (OCR approximate) |
| ~17:00–18:00 | Peak physical performance (strength, cardiovascular) |
| ~21:00 | Melatonin secretion begins |
| ~02:00 | Melatonin peak / deepest sleep |
| 22:00–02:00 | Primary deep sleep window [CONFIRMED — AUD-008] |

PMers: shift all times ~1–2 hours later.

### Light — the master lever [CONFIRMED — PPT-001, DOC-006, AUD-006]

Light is the primary environmental input to the circadian clock.

| Context | Lux | Role |
|---------|-----|------|
| Outdoors (clear day) | >100,000 | Reference maximum |
| Target morning exposure (ARP, cycle 1) | 10,000 | Serotonin trigger, clock reset |
| Target per cycle (cycles 2–10, Phases 1–2) | 1,000 | Sustained daytime activation |
| Diminished amber/red (evening, Phase 3) | ~500 | Melatonin zone — do not exceed |
| Indoor (middle of room) | ~500 | Typical daytime deficit |
| Indoor (by window) | ~3,000 | Better but not optimal |

Seasonal targets [CONFIRMED — PPT-001]:
- Standard day: 12h daylight / 4h amber / 8h dark
- April–September: 16h / 2h / 6h
- October–March: 8h / 8h / 8h — **SAD risk window**; light therapy mandatory

The morning activation chain (R90NRG): **ARP + 10,000 Lux (daylight or DWS device)** resets the inner clock and determines when melatonin will rise approximately 12 hours later. (P-023, P-066, DR-038)

### Polyphasic heritage [CONFIRMED — AUD-001, DOC-006]

Monophasic sleep (one nocturnal block) is a post-electric-light construct. Before the light bulb, humans slept polyphasically — biphasic, triphasic, or multiphasic. The R90 system deliberately restores polyphasic patterns through daytime CRPs and MRMs. (P-013, P-014)

## 5. Recovery Logic

### The anchor: ARP [CONFIRMED — DOC-003]

**ARP (Anchor Reset Point)** = the fixed, consistent daily wake time. The single most important scheduling decision. Never changes — not on weekends, not after short nights, not after travel. All 16 cycles, all sleep onset times, all CRP windows are derived from the ARP. (P-041, DR-018, DR-023)

Nick's personal ARP is 06:30. Whether 06:30 is also a common coaching recommendation or solely his personal example is **uncertain** (OQ-022).

### Sleep scheduling [CONFIRMED — DOC-002]

Count backwards from ARP in 90-minute increments to set sleep onset:
- 5 cycles: ARP minus 7.5h (e.g., 06:30 ARP → 23:00 sleep onset)
- 4 cycles: ARP minus 6h → 00:30
- 6 cycles: ARP minus 9h → 21:30

If you arrive home too late to hit your target onset: **wait for the next cycle**. Missing a cycle boundary and forcing sleep mid-cycle produces worse recovery than sleeping one cycle less at the right boundary. "Like missing a bus — catch the next one." (DR-036, DOC-006)

### CRP — Controlled Reset Period [CONFIRMED — PPT-001, AUD-003, DOC-006]

- **Duration:** 30 minutes (within a 90-minute cycle container)
- **Timing:** Phase 2, cycles 6/7/8 — midday to early evening
- **Requirement:** Does not require sleep. Mental disengagement is sufficient — "mind space time, mindset" (AUD-005)
- **Counts as:** One recovery cycle toward the 35/week target
- **Purpose:** Adds cycles to a short week; removes bedtime pressure; bridges chronotype misalignment
- **Early form (STALE):** Earlier sources (DOC-003, AUD-003) say "20–30 minutes" and "Controlled Recovery Period." Current preferred form (PPT-001): **30 minutes, Controlled Reset Period.**

### MRM — Micro Reset Moment [CONFIRMED — PPT-001, DOC-003]

- **Duration:** 3–5 minutes
- **Frequency:** 7 per day — one per active waking cycle (Phases 1, 2, and 3)
- **Requirement:** No sleep. Vacant mindspace — look out a window, refill water, step outside, change direction
- **Early form (STALE):** DOC-003 says "minimum 5 minutes" and "Micro Recovery Moment." Current preferred form (PPT-001): **3–5 minutes, Micro Reset Moment.**
- **Effect:** Even at minimum, measurably improves sleep onset, sleep maintenance, and deep sleep access

### Weekly cycle accounting [CONFIRMED — AUD-003]

The week, not the night, is the primary accounting unit. Track toward **35 cycles per week**. A 3-cycle night is not a failure — it is an input corrected by CRP and the following nights. This is the mechanism by which R90 removes nightly anxiety. (P-035, DR-020)

### Post-sleep routine [CONFIRMED — DOC-002, DOC-006]

The first 90-minute block after ARP is an active recovery/activation cycle with a fixed sequence:

**bladder → daylight (10,000 Lux) → hydrate → fuel up → mental challenge → exercise → bowels**

This sequence is unhurried and in order. The morning routine determines the quality of the entire day — and directly impacts the following night's recovery. "Post-sleep routine > pre-sleep routine" (P-047).

### Pre-sleep routine [CONFIRMED — AUD-004, DOC-006]

Executed in Phase 3, the final 90-minute waking cycle. Core requirements (non-negotiable):
- Shift to amber/red/dim light (~500 Lux)
- Allow room to cool (body slightly warmer than room)
- Download/declutter — resolve unfinished thoughts, close open loops
- Tech down — reduce stimulating device use

Personalisation is permitted **around** these core conditions — yoga, meditation, smells, sounds are additions, not replacements. (P-052, DR-028, DR-029, DR-030)

### The 15-minute rule [CONFIRMED — DOC-006]

If sleep onset does not occur within 15 minutes: get up, stay in chillout mode, wait for the next 90-minute cycle boundary. Do not lie anxious in bed. (P-067, DR-037)

### Sleep restriction (reset tactic) [CONFIRMED — AUD-008]

For persistent disrupted sleep: deliberately choose a later cycle entry time (e.g., shift 23:00 onset to 00:30 or 02:00) to consolidate sleep cycles. Compensate with daytime CRPs. Not about reducing hours — about increasing cycle depth at the correct circadian timing. (P-077, DR-045)

## 7. Environmental Rules

### The sleeping space as a recovery room [CONFIRMED — AUD-006, DOC-006]

The bedroom's primary association must be sleep and recovery — not work, entertainment, or technology. The mental model: strip the room to an empty shell and only return items that serve recovery. (P-072, DR-041)

### Familiarisation — the core mechanism [CONFIRMED — AUD-006]

The brain will not descend to deep sleep stages in an environment it does not recognise as safe. Familiarisation requires personalised sensory anchors:

- **Sound** — noise profile matching the person's formative environment
- **Smell** — essential oils, laundry scent, nature references, personal anchors
- **Visual** — calming, non-stimulating, nature-themed
- **Comfort objects** — childhood items or personal anchors; travel with the person

**Unfamiliar environments reduce recovery by up to 40% mentally and physically.** This is why athlete travel protocols exist. (P-071, P-072, DR-040)

### Light and temperature protocols [CONFIRMED — AUD-004, AUD-006, DOC-006]

**Morning (activation):**
- Dark room → light (daylight or 10,000 Lux DWS device)
- Warm body → cool environment

**Evening (deactivation):**
- Bright/blue light → amber/red/dim (~500 Lux)
- Cool ambient → warm (body slightly warmer than room, not hot)

**Blackout + Dawn Wake Simulator are a paired intervention** — neither works optimally without the other. Blackout removes the natural serotonin trigger; the DWS replaces it artificially. (P-073, DR-027, DR-028, DR-029, DR-039)

### Products and physical setup [CONFIRMED — AUD-007, DOC-006]

**Sleeping position:** Non-dominant side, foetal position. Right-handers on left side; left-handers on right. Biomechanical rationale (less joint pressure) + security signal (dominant side free to protect). (P-059, DR-031)

**Mattress:** Build in layers — customisable, replaceable, portable. If cheek-to-surface gap in foetal position > hand's width, add a layer. A correctly layered surface eliminates the need for pillows. (P-060, P-061, DR-032)

**Duvets:** Individual duvets for couples with different temperature preferences — eliminates one of the most common unacknowledged sleep disruptors. (P-062, DR-033)

**Breathing:** Nose breathing during sleep is the natural optimal state. Mouth breathing is common but disruptive — trainable. (P-063)

**Linen:** Fresh linen is a familiarisation trigger (cooler, fresher feel = "this is a good sleep environment"). Effect degrades by night 2. Target: **fresh linen every night** (PPT-001), or as frequently as practically achievable. (P-065, DR-034)

**Air quality:** Hypoallergenic bedding (synthetic often preferred over natural for allergen resistance), frequent washing, room airing. (P-064)

### Interventions — the framework principle [CONFIRMED — DOC-003, DOC-006, PPT-001]

Any single intervention (mattress, tracker, app, supplement, meditation) applied **without the structural framework** is at best ineffective and at worst counterproductive. Isolated interventions are the most common mistake Nick observes. The question to ask for any intervention: **Why? What? For how long?** (P-044, P-070, DR-026)

Sleep tracker data = guide only. Not a definitive performance criterion. Use contextually alongside how the person feels. (P-076, DR-044)

### Is the system ready for a decision engine?

**For core scheduling and daily structure: YES.**

The system has sufficient confirmed knowledge to build a decision engine that:
1. Sets an ARP and derives all cycle times from it
2. Identifies chronotype and adjusts performance windows
3. Schedules CRP placement (Phase 2, cycles 6–8)
4. Places MRMs across the active day (7 × 3–5 min)
5. Generates post-sleep and pre-sleep routines
6. Responds to common disruption events (short night, late arrival, 2–3am waking, failed onset)
7. Provides environment setup guidance (light, temperature, familiarisation, products)
8. Assesses and flags intervention misuse

**For full personalisation and clinical application: NOT YET.**

The engine would require PPT-002 (Elite Coaching Template) and DOC-007 (full Playbook content) before it could:
- Intake a new client and build a personalised profile
- Handle clinical sleep disorders beyond general R90 principles
- Apply the in-betweener identification protocol
- Generate detailed product selection recommendations
- Adapt robustly to shift workers and frequent travellers

**Recommended next step before starting engine design:**
Process PPT-002 and DOC-007 (the two highest-priority pending sources). Together they likely contain the profiling tool, the full coaching sequence, and the applied case study logic that would complete the personalisation layer.
