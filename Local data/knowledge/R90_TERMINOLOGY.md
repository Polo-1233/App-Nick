## Core Framework

### R90 Technique
- **Full form:** R90 Technique
- **Definition:** Nick Littlehales's named sleep methodology. Structures the entire 24-hour day around 90-minute cycles — not just night-time sleep. Turns sleep science into a practical daily system.
- **Source:** `transcripts/02_sleep_in_cycles_final.txt` · line 27
- **Confidence:** CONFIRMED
- **Notes:** "After years of working with top athletes, I developed an approach I call the R90 technique."

### KSRI
- **Full form:** Key Sleep Recovery Indicator
- **Definition:** The 7 pillars of Nick's R90 system. Each KSRI is an area of sleep/recovery knowledge that clients must understand and apply. The 7 KSRIs form the curriculum of the R90-T Playbook audio series.
- **Source:** `transcripts/Lesson_1.txt` · lines 1–2
- **Confidence:** CONFIRMED
- **Notes:** "It's a key sleep recovery indicator." Defined by Nick in the opening sentence of Lesson 1. 7 KSRIs: (1) Circadian Rhythm, (2) Chronotype, (3) Recovery Cycles, (4) Recovery Rhythm, (5) Environment, (6) Product Interventions, (7) Redefining Behaviour.

### SWR
- **Full form:** Sleep-Wake Routine
- **Definition:** The structured daily framework that organises all sleep and waking activity. Without a structured SWR, the ability to adapt and maximise recovery is severely limited.
- **Source:** `transcripts/02_sleep_in_cycles_final.txt` · line 5
- **Confidence:** CONFIRMED
- **Notes:** "Without knowledge and structure underpinning any sleep-wake routine (SWR)..."

### PB
- **Full form:** Personal Best
- **Definition:** The recoverable, consistent optimal performance state for each individual. The R90 methodology exists to make PB achievable more regularly — ideally every day. Borrowed from athletics; applied to daily life.
- **Source:** `transcripts/01_sleep_and_performance_final.txt` · lines 11, 15
- **Confidence:** CONFIRMED
- **Notes:** Nick uses "PB" to reframe elite performance as accessible to everyone, not just athletes.

### 1st Health Pillar / Health Pillar Framing
- **Full form:** Health Pillar (variable framing across sources)
- **Definition:** Nick uses multiple framings depending on context and source vintage:
  - **"1st Health Pillar"** (DOC-001): rhetorical emphasis — sleep comes first in the coaching conversation
  - **3-pillar model** (TXT-001, AUD-010): sleep + nutrition + exercise as three equal pillars. AUD-010: "two other natural health pillars to the human being, nutrition and exercise" — confirms sleep as one of three.
  - **4-pillar model** (DOC-007, PPT-002): sleepING + hydratING + eatING + exercisING — adds hydration as a fourth active pillar in the SleepING reframe context.
- **Source:** Multiple sources; see above
- **Confidence:** CONFIRMED (all framings are authentic; different contexts, not contradictions)
- **Notes:** OQ-003 CLOSED. The ranking question is resolved: sleep is parallel to, not strictly above, the other pillars. The "1st" language is entry-point emphasis for coaching conversations, not a scientific hierarchy.

## Recovery Units

### ARP
- **Full form:** Anchor Reset Point *(variant: Anchor Rise Point)*
- **Definition:** The fixed, consistent daily wake-up and start time. The master scheduling anchor from which all 16 daily 90-minute cycles are organised. Does not change — not on weekends, not after short nights.
- **Source:** `transcripts/03_sleep_recovery_final.txt` · line 29
- **Confidence:** CONFIRMED
- **Notes:** "Define your most consistent wake-up, start to your day time (ARP Anchor Reset Point)." Prior inference of "Anchor Recovery Point" was incorrect — corrected by DOC-003. Nick uses 6:30 as his personal ARP example across lessons. DOC-009 (internal structure document) uses "Anchor Rise Point" as an alternative expansion — likely an earlier or informal variant. Canonical form = "Anchor Reset Point."

### CRP
- **Full form:** Controlled Reset Period *(earlier form: Controlled Recovery Period; variant: Cycle Reset Period)*
- **Definition:** A deliberate 30-minute rest/mindspace period taken at midday or in the early evening (Phase 2, cycles 6/7/8). Counts as a recovery cycle toward the 35/week target. Does not require sleep — mental disengagement is sufficient. Nick rebrands "napping" as CRP to remove cultural stigma.
- **Source:** `transcripts/V2_R90-T_workshop_deck.txt` · slide 31, 52 (preferred form); `transcripts/Lesson_3.txt` · line 64–65; `transcripts/03_sleep_recovery_final.txt` · line 27; `transcripts/PDF-001_28-08_UK_PB_clean.txt` · pages 77–78
- **Confidence:** CONFIRMED
- **Notes:** Three full-form variants appear across sources — all abbreviated CRP: (1) "Controlled Recovery Period" (DOC-003, AUD-003 — earliest form); (2) "Controlled Reset Period" (PPT-001, DOC-006, DOC-007 — current coaching form); (3) "Cycle Reset Period" (PDF-001 page 78 body text — likely informal/editorial variant in published book; heading on same page 77 says "Controlled Reset Period"). **Canonical preferred form = "Controlled Reset Period".** Also: the published playbook (PDF-001, page 124) uses "Controlled Recovery Periods" as a third variant in sidebar text. Distinct from MRM (which is 3–5 min, no sleep required). **Duration three-tier menu (DOC-011):** 90 min (full recovery cycle — high-deficit or extended afternoon window), 30 min (standard CRP), 20 min (minimum viable). NSDR is now a formally sanctioned CRP format (DOC-005).

### MRM
- **Full form:** Micro Reset Moment *(earlier form: Micro Recovery Moment)*
- **Definition:** A 3–5 minute mental disengagement break taken every 90-minute waking cycle. Requires no sleep. Target: 7 MRMs per active day (one per waking cycle, across Phases 1–3). The lightest layer of the polyphasic recovery architecture. Even at minimum, measurably improves sleep onset, maintenance, and deep sleep access.
- **Source:** `transcripts/V2_R90-T_workshop_deck.txt` · slide 31, 52 (preferred form); `transcripts/03_sleep_recovery_final.txt` · line 27
- **Confidence:** CONFIRMED
- **Notes:** Current client-facing form = "Micro Reset Moment" (PPT-001, DOC-006). Earlier audio sources say "Micro Recovery Moment" (DOC-003). Specification: "7 x 3/5 minutes / Vacant Mindspace – Lighten Up." Distinct from CRP (which is 30 min).

### 16 Cycles Per Day
- **Full form:** Sixteen 90-Minute Cycles
- **Definition:** 24 hours ÷ 90 minutes = 16 cycles. Nick structures the entire day as 16 cycles — some for sleep, some for activity, some for recovery (CRP). This reframes the day as an integrated recovery system rather than "work + sleep."
- **Source:** `transcripts/02_sleep_in_cycles_final.txt` · line 29; `transcripts/03_sleep_recovery_final.txt` · line 29
- **Confidence:** CONFIRMED

### 5 Cycles Per Day / 35 Cycles Per Week
- **Full form:** Five 90-Minute Cycles / Thirty-Five Cycles Per Week
- **Definition:** The R90 daily target is 5 complete 90-minute cycles (= 7.5h equivalent). The weekly target is 35 cycles (5/day × 7 days). Weekly accounting allows short nights to be balanced with CRP — removing nightly anxiety.
- **Source:** `transcripts/Lesson_3.txt` · lines 18–20, 37
- **Confidence:** CONFIRMED
- **Notes:** "Five 90-minute cycles equals 7.5 hours." "Over a seven-day period of maybe 35 cycles in a week."

### 30% Rule
- **Full form:** Thirty Percent of the 24-Hour Period in Sleep State
- **Definition:** R90's proportional sleep target: approximately 30% of each 24-hour period should be spent in a sleep/recovery state. 30% × 24h ≈ 7.2h ≈ 5 cycles.
- **Source:** `transcripts/Lesson_3.txt` · lines 51–52
- **Confidence:** CONFIRMED

### Post-Sleep Period
- **Full form:** Post-Sleep Period / Post-Sleep Routine
- **Definition:** The sequenced, unhurried set of actions taken in the first 90-minute block after waking. Considered more impactful than the pre-sleep routine. Sequence: bladder → daylight → hydration → food → mental challenges → exercise → bowels.
- **Source:** `transcripts/02_sleep_in_cycles_final.txt` · lines 33–41; `transcripts/Lesson_4.txt` · lines 22–48
- **Confidence:** CONFIRMED
- **Notes:** "The key to overall sleep quality is what you do now." Nick confirms in AUD-004 that this is also KSRI 4's central focus.

### Staircase Metaphor
- **Full form:** Sleep Cycle Staircase
- **Definition:** Nick's metaphor for the 90-minute sleep cycle: top of the staircase = light sleep, bottom = deep/REM sleep. Only 20–25% of each cycle is accessible as deep or REM sleep. Unresolved food, fluid, or thoughts trap you near the top.
- **Source:** `transcripts/Lesson_4.txt` · lines 89–122
- **Confidence:** CONFIRMED
- **Notes:** "We're at the top of the stairs and at the bottom is all the good stuff... Unfortunately, there's only 20 to 25% in any particular period available to us."

## Chronobiology

### Circadian Rhythm
- **Full form:** Circadian Rhythm
- **Definition:** The 24-hour biological clock driven by light, dark, and temperature. Mediated by the pineal gland and hypothalamus. It governs when the body wants to sleep, peak alertness, physical performance windows, hormone production, and body temperature. External and internal clocks stay synchronised through environmental cues.
- **Source:** `transcripts/Lesson_1.txt` · lines 1–15
- **Confidence:** CONFIRMED

### Chronotype
- **Full form:** Chronotype
- **Definition:** A genetic, innate characteristic that determines natural sleep timing and daily performance patterns. Cannot be eliminated — only worked with or suppressed. Two primary types: AMer and PMer. A third emerging pattern: in-betweener.
- **Source:** `transcripts/Lesson_2.txt` · lines 1–3; `transcripts/Lesson_1.txt` · lines 73–78
- **Confidence:** CONFIRMED
- **Notes:** "A genetic true reflection of your sleep characteristic. Your natural sleep characteristics that dominate your everyday."

### AMer
- **Full form:** AM Chronotype (Morning Type / Lark)
- **Definition:** Naturally wakes 5–7am. Peak cognitive and physical performance in the morning. The pineal gland shifts from melatonin to serotonin faster at sunrise. Fades in the evening. ~30% of the population (Nick's professional estimate).
- **Source:** `transcripts/Lesson_2.txt` · lines 9–14, 31–32
- **Confidence:** CONFIRMED

### PMer
- **Full form:** PM Chronotype (Evening Type / Owl)
- **Definition:** Naturally active in the late afternoon/evening. The pineal gland's melatonin-to-serotonin shift is delayed ~1–2 hours behind an AMer. Prefers to wake 8–9am or later. Comfortable working late into the night. ~70% of the population (Nick's professional estimate).
- **Source:** `transcripts/Lesson_2.txt` · lines 50, 65–70
- **Confidence:** CONFIRMED
- **Notes:** "The PM chronotype, the nighttime chronotype, is a bit like a sort of phase delay. It's about one to two hours behind my process."

### In-Betweener
- **Full form:** In-Betweener Chronotype
- **Definition:** A person whose natural chronotype has been blurred by lifestyle, occupation, and social habits. Camouflages chronotype signals with stimulants (caffeine, supplements) to function in a mismatched schedule.
- **Source:** `transcripts/Lesson_2.txt` · lines 22–25
- **Confidence:** CONFIRMED
- **Notes:** "There's a large percentage of people who are now becoming what's called in-betweeners." Whether this is a formally defined R90 category or an observed phenomenon is unresolved (OQ-021).

### Phase Delay
- **Full form:** Circadian Phase Delay
- **Definition:** The PMer's biological lag: their melatonin-to-serotonin shift occurs ~1–2 hours later than an AMer's. All circadian performance windows are shifted correspondingly later.
- **Source:** `transcripts/Lesson_2.txt` · lines 65–67
- **Confidence:** CONFIRMED

### Polyphasic Sleep
- **Full form:** Polyphasic Sleep
- **Definition:** Sleeping in shorter periods more often throughout the 24-hour period, rather than one single block. The natural human sleep pattern pre-electric light. Includes biphasic, triphasic, and multiphasic variants.
- **Source:** `transcripts/Lesson_1.txt` · lines 21–23
- **Confidence:** CONFIRMED

### Monophasic Sleep
- **Full form:** Monophasic Sleep
- **Definition:** A single continuous sleep block at night. The post-industrial default. Nick frames this as an unnatural modern construct — not the biological norm.
- **Source:** `transcripts/Lesson_1.txt` · line 44
- **Confidence:** CONFIRMED

### Urgent Need
- **Full form:** Urgent Need (Sleep Drive)
- **Definition:** Nick's term for the biological sleep drive — the point in the circadian cycle when the body signals a compulsion to recover. Distinct from general tiredness or exhaustion; framed as a scheduled biological signal.
- **Source:** `transcripts/Lesson_1.txt` · line 17
- **Confidence:** STRONG INFERENCE
- **Notes:** "It creates a series of things like when we want to go to sleep, when we want to recover. It's called urgent need." Term used once; not elaborated. Likely relates to the 90-minute cycle boundary signal.

## Sleep Physiology

### SWS
- **Full form:** Slow Wave Sleep
- **Definition:** Deep sleep — Stage 3 of the sleep cycle. During SWS, growth hormone is released. Longer SWS periods correlate with better daytime wakefulness. SWS deprivation causes increased daytime sleepiness and reduced athletic performance.
- **Source:** `transcripts/03_sleep_recovery_final.txt` · line 15
- **Confidence:** CONFIRMED
- **Notes:** "Slow Wave Sleep (SWS) or deep sleep as it's more commonly known, is vital for optimum recovery in athletes."

### Melatonin
- **Full form:** Melatonin
- **Definition:** Hormone produced by the pineal gland. Tells the brain to suppress all bodily functions and move toward nocturnal sleep. Triggered by darkness and diminishing light. Suppressed by morning serotonin.
- **Source:** `transcripts/Lesson_2.txt` · lines 55–57
- **Confidence:** CONFIRMED

### Serotonin
- **Full form:** Serotonin
- **Definition:** Hormone produced by the pineal gland. Tells the brain to unsuppress bodily functions and become active. Triggered by morning light/sunrise. Described as the "get active" hormone. The AMer's pineal gland produces it faster than the PMer's.
- **Source:** `transcripts/Lesson_2.txt` · lines 58–59; `transcripts/02_sleep_in_cycles_final.txt` · line 37
- **Confidence:** CONFIRMED

### Pineal Gland
- **Full form:** Pineal Gland
- **Definition:** A brain gland that produces both melatonin (sleep onset) and serotonin (activation). The site of the melatonin-to-serotonin shift that determines chronotype. Responds to light cues relayed from the retina.
- **Source:** `transcripts/Lesson_1.txt` · lines 8–9; `transcripts/Lesson_2.txt` · lines 54–59
- **Confidence:** CONFIRMED

### Hypothalamus
- **Full form:** Hypothalamus
- **Definition:** The brain region that houses the internal body clock (suprachiasmatic nucleus, implied). Works alongside the pineal gland to synchronise the circadian rhythm with external light/dark cues.
- **Source:** `transcripts/Lesson_1.txt` · lines 8–9
- **Confidence:** CONFIRMED

## Pathological / Behavioural Terms

### Electronic Insomnia
- **Full form:** Electronic Insomnia
- **Definition:** Nick's term for sleep disruption driven by screens, gaming, social media, and virtual communication — creating a "can't switch it off" mindset. Classified as a distinct, modern pathology separate from conventional insomnia. Rapidly rising, especially in athletes.
- **Source:** `transcripts/03_sleep_recovery_final.txt` · line 5
- **Confidence:** CONFIRMED
- **Notes:** "what we call Electronic Insomnia, classified as gamers, social networkers, and virtual communicators."

### Ortho-Insomnia
- **Full form:** Ortho-Insomnia
- **Definition:** Sleep anxiety caused by consumer sleep tracker data. The paradox: using a tracker to improve sleep creates anxiety about the data, which worsens sleep. Nick classifies it as a new and growing modern disorder, distinct from Electronic Insomnia and classical insomnia.
- **Source:** `transcripts/Lesson_8.txt` · lines 304–333
- **Confidence:** CONFIRMED
- **Notes:** "There is another one coming along, which is called ortho insomnia. And this is because technology has come into our lives and we're able to start measuring sleep for the first time ever... looking at sleep data can actually add more worry to the process." Sleep trackers should be treated as a guide only, not a definitive performance criterion.

### Familiarisation
- **Full form:** Sensory Familiarisation
- **Definition:** The psychological mechanism by which the brain recognises an environment as safe for deep sleep entry. Built through consistent exposure to specific sensory cues: sounds, smells, visual elements, comfort objects. When familiarisation cues are absent (unfamiliar environment), recovery can drop by up to 40% mentally and physically.
- **Source:** `transcripts/Lesson_6.txt` · lines 153–157
- **Confidence:** CONFIRMED
- **Notes:** "This is all about familiarisation. This is all about the space that you're choosing to sleep in and how you can familiarize that place with some of the things that are very important to you." Central concept of KSRI 6 (Environment lesson).

## Extended Framework (from DOC-006)

### Four Phases of the Day
- **Full form:** Four Phases of the 24-Hour Period
- **Definition:** Nick divides the 24-hour day into 4 phases aligned with the circadian rhythm:
  - **Phase 1 (Sunrise → Midday):** Cycles 1–4 — activation, high-intensity activity, MRMs
  - **Phase 2 (Midday → Early Evening):** Cycles 5–8 — CRP window (cycles 6/7/8), continued activity, MRMs
  - **Phase 3 (Evening → Sleep onset):** Cycles 8–11/12 — wind-down, pre-sleep preparation, MRMs
  - **Phase 4 (Nocturnal sleep):** Cycles 11/12–16 — 3–6 sleeping cycles (typically 5 for standard day)
- **Source:** `transcripts/R90-T_Playbook_Overview_.txt` · lines 280, 446, 600–606
- **Confidence:** CONFIRMED
- **Notes:** "It's all about the four phases in every 24 hours — Sunrise — Midday — Sunset — Night." Resolves OQ-024.

### SleepING
- **Full form:** SleepING (active reframe of "sleep")
- **Definition:** Nick's deliberate reframing — adding "ING" to sleep repositions it as an active, daily health pillar alongside hydrating, eating, exercising, and socialising. Shifts the mindset from passive to proactive.
- **Source:** `transcripts/R90-T_Playbook_Overview_.txt` · lines 89–95
- **Confidence:** CONFIRMED
- **Notes:** "Put an ING on it! We are going sleepING..." Core coaching technique for attitude change.

### Sleep Noise
- **Full form:** Sleep Noise
- **Definition:** Nick's term for the increasing media- and algorithm-driven "sleep fear factor" — constant warnings about how important sleep is that paradoxically create anxiety and worsen sleep. Device Dipping is the mechanism that worsens it.
- **Source:** `transcripts/R90-T_Playbook_Overview_.txt` · lines 214, 226–228
- **Confidence:** CONFIRMED
- **Notes:** "Be mindful of the increasing sleep fear factor in play, constantly reminding us, why it's so important and we better get it right every night or else!" Previously listed as NEEDS VALIDATION; now confirmed.

### Social Jet Lag
- **Full form:** Social Jet Lag
- **Definition:** Biological misalignment experienced by PMers forced to operate on AMer-structured schedules (early start times, morning culture). Caused by occupational and social demands, not travel. Leads to stimulant reliance and chronic under-performance.
- **Source:** `transcripts/R90-T_Playbook_Overview_.txt` · line 358
- **Confidence:** CONFIRMED
- **Notes:** "We mask our true characteristics with alarm clocks, overactivity and over-stimulation, leading to what's referred to as 'social jet lag'."

### Device Dipping
- **Full form:** Device Dipping
- **Definition:** The behaviour of searching for sleep solutions online when under sleep pressure — which triggers sleep-content recommendation algorithms, deepening anxiety rather than resolving it. A self-reinforcing component of Sleep Noise.
- **Source:** `transcripts/R90-T_Playbook_Overview_.txt` · lines 216–218
- **Confidence:** CONFIRMED

### Dawn Wake Simulator (DWS)
- **Full form:** Dawn Wake Simulator
- **Definition:** A bedroom light therapy device that gradually increases light intensity before the ARP, simulating sunrise and waking the user naturally without an abrupt alarm. Produces 10,000 Lux at full intensity.
- **Source:** `transcripts/R90-T_Playbook_Overview_.txt` · lines 526, 630
- **Confidence:** CONFIRMED

### R90NRG
- **Full form:** R90 Energy Formula
- **Definition:** Nick's formula for the morning activation chain: ARP + Daylight (Blue light) — 10,000 Lux device. The sequence that energises the day and determines night sleep quality.
- **Source:** `transcripts/R90-T_Playbook_Overview_.txt` · line 536
- **Confidence:** CONFIRMED

## Sleep Products / Physical Environment

### Foetal Position (Non-Dominant Side)
- **Full form:** Non-Dominant Side Foetal Sleeping Position
- **Definition:** Nick's recommended default sleeping position: foetal (curled), on the side opposite to the dominant hand. Biomechanical rationale: less joint/muscle pressure on non-dominant side. Security rationale: dominant side remains free to protect, signalling to the brain it is safe to enter deep sleep.
- **Source:** `transcripts/Lesson_7.txt` · lines 28–38; `transcripts/R90-T_Playbook_Overview_.txt` · lines 790–794
- **Confidence:** CONFIRMED
- **Notes:** Right-handers sleep on left side. Nick describes this as his lifelong personal practice and a consistent coaching recommendation.

### Mattress Check (Hands-Width Gap Test)
- **Full form:** Mattress Surface Check
- **Definition:** Field diagnostic for mattress suitability: (1) Lie on the floor in foetal position on non-dominant side. (2) Observe the gap between cheek and floor. (3) Repeat on actual mattress. (4) If gap exceeds a hand's width, the mattress is too firm and "asking you to use pillows." Remedy: add a soft layer.
- **Source:** `transcripts/Lesson_7.txt` · lines 159–186
- **Confidence:** CONFIRMED

### 15-Minute Rule
- **Full form:** Sleep Onset 15-Minute Rule
- **Definition:** If sleep has not come within 15 minutes of lying down: get up, remain in a chillout mode, wait for the next 90-minute cycle boundary, then try again. Prevents anxiety from lying in bed awake.
- **Source:** `transcripts/R90-T_Playbook_Overview_.txt` · lines 680, 854
- **Confidence:** CONFIRMED

## New Terms (from TXT-001)

### Junk Sleep
- **Full form:** Junk Sleep
- **Definition:** Prof. Chris Idzikowski's term for poor-quality, disrupted, shorter sleep caused by technology and lifestyle factors preventing melatonin production and disrupting the circadian process. Characterised by electronic device use before bed and exposure to blue-spectrum light in the melatonin window.
- **Source:** `Data Brut/Art de mieux dormir, L_ - Nick Littlehales.txt` · Chapter 1 (TXT-001)
- **Confidence:** CONFIRMED
- **Notes:** Nick adopts this term from Prof. Idzikowski. Closely related to Electronic Insomnia (BP-020) but framed from the quality/mechanism angle rather than the behavioural pattern.

### Morphotype
- **Full form:** Body Morphotype (sleep surface profiling)
- **Definition:** Nick's classification of body types for mattress surface selection: (1) **Ectomorph** — slim, narrow, long limbs; least cushioning needed. (2) **Mesomorph** — intermediate build; mid-range cushioning. (3) **Endomorph** — larger frame, broad hips/shoulders; most cushioning needed. For couples: the dominant morphotype determines the surface profile (endomorph > mesomorph > ectomorph). The hands-gap test (6 cm threshold) is simpler and takes priority over self-classification.
- **Source:** `Data Brut/Art de mieux dormir, L_ - Nick Littlehales.txt` · Chapter 6 (TXT-001)
- **Confidence:** CONFIRMED
- **Notes:** Not in any previously processed source. A practical tool for mattress selection when the hands-gap test result is ambiguous.

### Montmorency Cherry
- **Full form:** Montmorency (Tart) Cherry
- **Definition:** A food-based sleep intervention with clinical evidence. Research by Prof. Glyn Howatson (Northumbria University) shows Montmorency (tart) cherries increase melatonin production and measurably improve sleep duration and quality. Available dried or as juice. Nick's only explicitly named food-as-intervention beyond general tryptophan foods.
- **Source:** `Data Brut/Art de mieux dormir, L_ - Nick Littlehales.txt` · Chapter 8 (TXT-001)
- **Confidence:** CONFIRMED

### Tryptophan Foods
- **Full form:** Tryptophan-Rich Foods
- **Definition:** Foods containing the amino acid tryptophan, which the body uses to synthesise serotonin and then melatonin. Recommended for regular inclusion in the diet: chicken, turkey, dairy products, fish, bananas, nuts. A dietary support layer for natural melatonin production — not a replacement for the circadian light protocol.
- **Source:** `Data Brut/Art de mieux dormir, L_ - Nick Littlehales.txt` · Chapter 8 (TXT-001)
- **Confidence:** CONFIRMED

### Homeostat (Sleep Pressure)
- **Full form:** Homeostatic Sleep Pressure
- **Definition:** The biological drive to sleep that builds continuously from the moment of waking. The longer a person remains awake, the stronger the homeostatic pressure to sleep. Works alongside the circadian rhythm — when both align (circadian desire to sleep + high homeostatic pressure), sleep onset is rapid and deep. Circadian rhythms can temporarily override homeostatic pressure ("second wind" late at night).
- **Source:** `Data Brut/Art de mieux dormir, L_ - Nick Littlehales.txt` · Chapter 1 (TXT-001)
- **Confidence:** CONFIRMED

## DOC-007 Terms (from R90 Playbook)

### R90NRG

### In-betweener (formal description)
- **Full form:** In-betweener (R90 chronotype category)
- **Definition:** The third chronotype — "pretty much wired and tired all the time." Nick compares the in-betweener to a dolphin: "always active, even sleeping on the go." Falls between the typical morning (AMer) and evening (PMer) types. No fixed phase preference.
- **Source:** `Data Brut/IP Docs/R90 Playbook/28-08 PB clean content.docx` · KSPI 2 (DOC-007)
- **Confidence:** CONFIRMED
- **Notes:** No formal diagnostic test exists. The in-betweener is partly genetic, partly lifestyle-driven. PPT-002 notes that many people self-identify as in-betweeners due to occupational demands rather than true genetic chronotype.

### AMer / PMer (extended definitions)
- **Full form:** AMer (Morning Type) / PMer (Evening Type)
- **Definition (DOC-007 + PDF-001 additions):**
  - AMer: "Wake up early, love mornings and breakfast. Thrive in phases 1 and 2, fade in phase 3. Not great with night shifts or time zone travel." Old labels: larks, bears.
  - PMer: "Prefer to wake later, skip breakfast, put up with phases 1 and 2, love phase 3 and into phase 4. Happier with shifts and time zone travel." Old labels: lions, wolves. May have two ARPs (natural + occupational).
- **Source:** `Data Brut/IP Docs/R90 Playbook/28-08 PB clean content.docx` · KSPI 2 (DOC-007); `transcripts/PDF-001_28-08_UK_PB_clean.txt` · pages 51, 54 (PDF-001)
- **Confidence:** CONFIRMED
- **Notes (PDF-001 addition):** The contrast is now explicit and confirmed in the published playbook: AMers are actively worse at time zone travel and night shifts; PMers are actively better at both. App implication: travel/shift recommendations should account for chronotype adaptability.

### 15-Minute Rule
- **Full form:** 15-Minute Sleep Onset Rule
- **Definition:** If you cannot enter a sleep state within 15 minutes, stop trying. Get up. Stay in a low-stimulation chillout phase. Wait for the next 90-minute cycle boundary. Return to bed at that point.
- **Source:** `Data Brut/IP Docs/R90 Playbook/28-08 PB clean content.docx` · KSPI 4 (DOC-007)
- **Confidence:** CONFIRMED
- **Notes:** The circuit breaker for sleep onset failure. Prevents the anxiety feedback loop from compounding. Consistent with clinical sleep restriction logic — extended wakefulness in bed trains the brain that bed ≠ sleep.

### "Miss the Bus"
- **Full form:** Miss the Bus (cycle missed metaphor)
- **Definition:** Nick's metaphor for missing a planned sleep cycle start time. If you planned to sleep at 23:00 but are not ready until 23:30, do not go to bed at 23:30 — wait for the next cycle boundary (00:30). Going to bed mid-cycle delivers an incomplete, low-quality cycle.
- **Source:** `Data Brut/IP Docs/R90 Playbook/28-08 PB clean content.docx` · KSPI 3/4 (DOC-007)
- **Confidence:** CONFIRMED

### No-Worries Reality Actuals
- **Full form:** No-Worries Reality Actuals (weekly floor formula)
- **Definition:** Nick's term for the minimum acceptable weekly recovery standard — the floor below the ideal. Formula: **28 nocturnal cycles + 35 MRMs + 5 CRPs** per 7 days. The ideal remains 35 cycles + 42 MRMs + 7 CRPs.
- **Source:** `Data Brut/IP Docs/R90 Playbook/28-08 PB clean content.docx` · KSPI 7 templates (DOC-007)
- **Confidence:** CONFIRMED

### 30% Rule
- **Full form:** 30% Sleep Allocation Rule
- **Definition:** Ideally, ≥30% of the 24-hour period should be allocated to sleeping and recovery — approximately 8+ hours. Includes nocturnal cycles + CRP + MRMs. Very few achieve this consistently, but it sets the directional target.
