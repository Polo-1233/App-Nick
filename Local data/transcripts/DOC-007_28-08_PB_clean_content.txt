[0299][Normal] Polyphasic v Monophasic
[0300][Normal] Up until we invented electric light (the light bulb), not that long ago, we have evidence that as humans we would adopt a polyphasic approach to our sleep wake cycles (SWC). That means we were gathering our sleep and overall recovery in multiple periods throughout every 24 hours, common ones being bi-phasic (twice a day), tri-phasic (three times a day), and even up to six times a day.
[0302][Normal] Monophasic
[0303][Normal] One nocturnal block of hours. It was at this “light bulb” moment, when we shifted how we roll through our natural (circadian) rhythms in every 24 hours and became monophasic sleepers! Principally sleeping and recovering in just one nocturnal block, and the emerging mindset of you had better get your full eight hours a night!

[0399][Normal] What’s Worth Knowing
[0401][Normal] To understand our natural rhythms, let's go off-grid. Picture us on an uninhabited island, living by sunlight and firelight, hunting and gathering our food. As the last embers fade, we yawn and curl up under the stars. When dawn breaks, the birds sing us awake. We rise slowly, empty our bladders, eat, and go about our day. No rushing, no artificial light — just following the ancient patterns etched in our DNA.
[0403][Normal] That's how our internal master clock keeps us ticking. Bright morning daylight, especially blue light, halts production of the sleep hormone melatonin and signals a rise in serotonin to boost mood and alertness. As daylight fades, the process reverses: melatonin rises, easing us towards sleep, peaking in the small hours when the deepest sleep stages occur.
[0405][Normal] But when an isolated island retreat-like existence isn't on the cards, you can still take some simple steps to help your inner clock resync.
