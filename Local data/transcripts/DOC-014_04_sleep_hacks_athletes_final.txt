4. Sleep hacking techniques (elite athletes worldwide)

There are countless ways we try to guarantee a good sleep at night, such as not eating too late, spraying lavender fragrances on our pillows, or taking sleeping aid pills.

Typically, it doesn’t work because our brains are too wired from our busy, stressful lives – and have not been getting enough human centric recovery TLC throughout the day (i.e. exposure to natural sunlight (Blue Light), a break from smart devices etc).


For athletes fighting for the smallest increments of improvements in their personal best, this can mean the difference between being in play for a medal, or not being part of the “first team”. This is why many of them have adopted the following principles, or “hacks” if you so will. 


In contrast to most people, professional athletes have the space and freedom to decide how to break up their day, at least for the most part. You, too, should start with not trying to squeeze sleep into a busy schedule, making do with what’s left over after career, family, friends, and hobbies. Instead, ask yourself the question: 


What would my day look like if I had full control over my time?

Find out, what your chronotype is. In general terms, your chronotype defines whether you are a morning person or a night person – or an in-betweener, who doesn’t really prioritize either one. If you have a clear preference, build your daily routines around that. Or if you have to be in the office (or the training ground) at a particular day on some days, make this the defining ARP anchor reset time: set your alarm early enough for you to get up and have sufficient time for a healthy morning routine. 

This time let’s say it’s 6:30 in the morning, your ARP anchor reset point, in your rolling 24 hour plan to optimise mental and physical recovery. Break your day up into 16 x 90-minute-cycles to redefine your approach.

ARP 06.30 in every 24 hours rolling: 

Six 90 minute Cycles from 22.30pm into 06.30 = 9 Hours 

*Five 90 minute Cycles from 23.00pm into 06.30 = 7.5 Hours

Four 90 minute Cycles from 24.30pm into 06.30 = 6 Hours

For example adopting a daily five cycles nocturnal approach will give you 35 cycles every rolling seven days. Professional athletes, players coming back from a match, event sometimes have to pick a later cycle (asleep point) due to travel times but will either compensate, adapt with a short 30 minute CRP micro-break halfway through the day or an additional cycle a day later. 

Once you have those two important pillars, start overlaying your actual occupational and lifestyle schedule over this cycle. Pay extra attention to what you do during the first cycle after waking up. For some athletes, this is more significant than what they do last thing in the evening. 

Hydrate sufficiently first, then look for a source of bright light. Normal lights won’t do, it has to either be natural sunlight or a daylight simulation lamp. Eat something and do some light exercise. The point here is to let your inner clock, your circadian rhythm, know your plans for the day as well. 

Athletes will usually schedule their ARP anchor point based on the first training session of the day. Individual training sessions, physio, cryo-sessions, and the likes will be planned into suitable cycles throughout the day. 

Plan in short recovery periods throughout the day, but don’t make it too complicated. Use what comes natural to you. If you enjoy meditation, plan a short sequence in. Or a short walk. Or even a nap. 

Some athletes I know have turned to a newer approach called NSDR (Non-sleep deep rest). I’ll write in more detail in the final instalment of this series about this point. 

And finally, the evening routine. This has been widely covered in the media, but just one word. If you get the rest of your day right, watching a film in the evening is not going to have that much of a negative impact. However, avoid screen time directly before switching off the light. If you can, dim down the lights in the house in the last cycle to facilitate the production of melatonin, your sleep hormone, in your system.
