Deep sleep:

To understand our natural rhythms, let's go off-grid. Picture us on an uninhabited island, living by sunlight and firelight, hunting and gathering our food. As the last embers fade, we yawn and curl up under the stars. When dawn breaks, the birds sing us awake. We rise slowly, empty our bladders, eat, and go about our day. That's how our internal master clock keeps us ticking. Bright morning daylight, especially blue light, halts production of the sleep hormone melatonin and signals a rise in serotonin to boost mood and alertness. As daylight fades, the process reverses: melatonin rises, easing us towards sleep, peaking in the small hours when the deepest sleep stages occur.

Polyphasic v Monophasic

Up until we invented electric light (the light bulb), not that long ago, we have evidence that as humans we would adopt a polyphasic approach to our sleep wake cycles (SWC). That means we were gathering our sleep and overall recovery in multiple periods throughout every 24 hours, common ones being bi-phasic (twice a day), tri-phasic (three times a day), and even up to six times a day.

Monophasic

One nocturnal block of hours. It was at this “light bulb” moment, when we shifted how we roll through our natural (circadian) rhythms in every 24 hours and became monophasic sleepers! Principally sleeping and recovering in just one nocturnal block, and the emerging mindset of you had better get your full eight hours a night!

Four Stages of Sleep

Our brains develop four stages of sleep, each of which plays a key role in enabling a full recovery process and preparing us for our next 24-hour activities, overall health and well-being.

Deeper Sleep Stages

Non-REM (NREM) stage 3 and REM stage 4 are key restorative stages, usually being developed by our brains during the first three 90-minute cycles of our sleep, accounting for up to 25% of our total sleep time.

There are some very important processes being attended to by your brain when you reach stage 3 and 4. I won’t list them, just emphasise how important it is to do everything you can during your wake hours to help your brain develop them naturally.

Understanding Sleeping Cycles

Think of your sleeping as a journey up and down the stairs. You stand at the top awake, PJs on, teeth brushed, then gradually move down (asleep) through the four different stages of sleep.

NREM Stage 1

Light transitioning from a wake into sleep state

NREM Stage 2

Slow wave heart rate and body temp drops

NREM Stage 3

Deepest shutdown, blood pressure, breathing, and muscles relax

REM Stage 4

Body immobilised, brain active, rapid eye movement, and dreams

It’s a journey your brain controls, and ideally, you travel down the stairs through these stages and reaching the bottom and enjoy the good stuff in stages 3 & 4 for a short while before heading back up to start another cycle.

We can’t expect to enjoy stages 3 & 4 if we have asked too much of our brains from the moment of wake and throughout the day.

If we think of an eight hours sleeping target, but in cycles, that would be five 90-minute cycles and 7.5 hours with 15 minutes in and out. With this refocus, we now understand that it’s not all about the time we allocate, but have we done anything leading up to it, that helps us get to the bottom of the stairs naturally during our first three 90-minute cycles because thereafter cycles 4 & 5 are about lighter sleep stages and readiness to wake.

And of course, what can you avoid taking with you into these cycles that will make it more difficult? Worry, anxiety, stress, overthinking plans, a full stomach, over-hydrating, bladder/bowel movements, external noise, too hot, too cold, temperature, light — your 16-cycle-tactics will help you manage these disruptors!

Disturbed sleep

The common outcomes are not being able to get into sleep, staying asleep and waking up after maybe two or three cycles (02:00 03:00) and worrying about not being able to go back to sleep.

It’s a bit old school but it works:
