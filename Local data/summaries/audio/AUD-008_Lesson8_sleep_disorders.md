## Common Problems Covered

### 1. Sleep Apnea
- Breathing disorder; may require clinical testing and CPAP or similar equipment.
- "A very common problem around the world."
- Often only identified when a partner observes it.
- Nick's coverage is brief — refers to clinical specialists; not a focus of R90 intervention.

### 2. Snoring / Mouth Breathing
- Extremely common; often unnoticed when sleeping alone.
- Cause and solution directly connect to prior lessons: nose breathing, correct sleeping position (non-dominant foetal), appropriate mattress surface.
- Mouth breathing → dry mouth, dehydration, airway disruption → awakenings and reduced cycle access.

### 3. Overheating
- "Overheating and perspiring heavily is one of those things that we try and control." (lines 23–30)
- Caused by: partner heat, shared duvet, inability to control room temperature.
- Solution: room temperature management (AUD-004, AUD-006), individual duvets (AUD-007).

### 4. Racing Thoughts
- "Thoughts are something that we have to deal with throughout the day." (line 34)
- R90's direct solution: the full-day structure (MRMs + CRP + post-sleep period) is specifically designed to process information during waking hours so it doesn't carry into sleep.
- Visualisation and environment cues (river analogy) are secondary tools.
- Pre-sleep warm→cool / light→dark transition actively reduces serotonin and builds melatonin — the physiological route to thought settling.

### 5. Awakenings
- Night awakenings are common and not inherently pathological.
- Causes: bladder, overheating, thoughts, partner disturbance.
- All addressed through prior-lesson protocols.
- Frequent awakenings are partly a consequence of monophasic sleep — longer unbroken nocturnal blocks create more interruption opportunities.

### 6. 2–3am Wakefulness (Polyphasic Heritage)

**First explicit time window for deep sleep stages:**
> "Between sort of 10pm in the evening and 2am in the morning, within that natural process, is when the human is more likely to have achieved those deeper sleep stages. From the point of around 2 o'clock, 3 o'clock onwards... you're unlikely to be in those deeper sleep stages in general terms." (lines 78–84)

This explains why 2–3am awakening is so common: it occurs naturally at the transition out of the deep sleep phase — the body has completed the primary restorative work and shifts toward lighter cycling.

**R90 response to 2–3am wakefulness:**
- Do not try to force sleep.
- "Chill out. Don't overstimulate. Do some activities that are positive. You can watch things. You can read things. You can listen to things... And then the sleep onset will come along and maybe you can grab a couple of cycles in before your normal, consistent wake time." (lines 91–99)
- This is framed as a polyphasic heritage pattern — historically normal, not a disorder.

### 7. Difficulty Getting to Sleep
- Result of insufficient daytime preparation — the full-day structure was not in place.
- Serotonin still too high at bedtime = pre-sleep light/temperature transition was inadequate.
- Solution: address from point of wake forward; not fixable in the pre-sleep window alone.

### 8. Waking Too Early
- "Just chill out. Don't overstimulate. Don't try and go back to sleep. Just let the process go along until the 6:30 time comes along, your consistent wake time." (lines 120–126)
- Same principle as 2–3am: no anxiety; wait for the ARP; don't force re-entry into sleep.
- If it happens a full cycle early (e.g., 05:00 when ARP is 06:30), treat the time as a quiet chillout phase before the day starts.

### 9. Ortho-Insomnia (NEW CONFIRMED TERM)

> "There is another one coming along, which is called ortho insomnia. And this is because technology has come into our lives and we're able to start measuring sleep for the first time ever... When we start to bring things in with an uneducated approach, things like technology and looking at sleep data can actually add more worry to the process." (lines 304–333)

**Ortho-insomnia** = anxiety about sleep caused by sleep tracker data. A new and growing pathology driven by consumer sleep technology. Nick's position: trackers are "a guide only" — sleep data is not definitive and should not be used as a primary performance criterion.

This is the third sleep pathology Nick names:
1. **Electronic Insomnia** — screen/gaming/social-media-driven (DOC-003)
2. **Ortho-Insomnia** — tracker-data-driven (AUD-008)
3. Sleep anxiety as the primary disruptor (recurring across all sources)

## Sleep Restriction as a Reset Tactic

A specific intervention for disrupted sleep patterns:

> "In my particular case with my consistent wake time of 6:30 and a four-cycle routine from 12:30 into 6:30, maybe I'll choose 2am or even 3:30, the next cycles along, to restrict my sleep a little better, to try and reset my process balanced with the CRPs throughout the day." (lines 232–244)

**Sleep restriction** = deliberately choosing a later cycle entry time to consolidate sleep cycles and reset the circadian signal. Combined with daytime CRPs to compensate for cycle reduction. Nick applies this to his own routine as a practical recovery tool.
