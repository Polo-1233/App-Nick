## 2. Main Ideas

1. Post-sleep routine (morning) is more impactful than pre-sleep routine (evening)
2. The "staircase" metaphor: each 90-min cycle is a descent toward deep/REM sleep — only 20–25% is accessible
3. Anything still processing (food, fluid, thoughts) prevents descent — you get "stuck in light sleep"
4. Two environmental polarity pairs govern the daily rhythm:
   - Morning: warm → cool, dark → light (activation)
   - Evening: light → dark, cool → warm (deactivation)
5. Bedroom temperature: body should be a couple of degrees warmer than the room — not cold, not hot
6. Evening light type matters: amber/yellow/red light allows melatonin to build
7. Post-sleep routine is especially critical for PMers waking in an AMer world

## 3. R90 Principles Extracted

**P-047** [CONFIRMED — AUD-004]
**Post-sleep routine is more important than pre-sleep routine.** Nick explicitly challenges the focus on bedtime rituals: "Why do we wake up in the morning and just expect that whatever we've been doing all day long, we're going to be able to put right just before we go to bed?" The morning routine sets the conditions for everything that follows — including the quality of the next night's sleep.

**P-048** [CONFIRMED — AUD-004]
**The staircase metaphor.** Each 90-minute cycle is a staircase: top = light sleep, bottom = deep sleep (REM + non-REM). The goal each cycle is to descend to the bottom and return. Only **20–25% of each cycle** is accessible as deep/REM sleep — the rest is transitional. Everything that disrupts the descent (food, fluid, thoughts, worry) keeps you stuck at the top.

**P-049** [CONFIRMED — AUD-004]
**Causes of shallow sleep (stuck at the top of the stairs):**
- Still processing food or fluids → toilet urgency disrupts descent
- Unfinished thoughts, worries, unresolved items → brain cannot switch off
- Inadequate preparation during the day → no "download" of mental load before bed
The result: you recover, but less well than you could.

**P-050** [CONFIRMED — AUD-004]
**Morning polarity pair — activation:**
- **Warm → cool** (body temperature comes down to meet the environment)
- **Dark → light** (light triggers serotonin, starts the day)
These two environmental shifts are the essential triggers for the post-sleep activation. "If you don't have those things in that first hour or 90 minutes of your day, you're always going to be going throughout your day not necessarily at your best or at your peak."

**P-051** [CONFIRMED — AUD-004]
**Evening polarity pair — deactivation:**
- **Light → dark** (shift to amber/yellow/red light to allow melatonin build)
- **Cool → warm** (body temperature slightly warmer than the bedroom signals sleep onset)
These mirror the morning process in reverse — the circadian cycle is a loop of two opposing environmental states.

**P-052** [CONFIRMED — AUD-004]
**Bedroom temperature rule:** Body should be **a couple of degrees warmer than the bedroom** — not cold, not a hot room. A quick shower or light exercise/yoga before bed achieves the slight body warming needed. This differential signals the brain to initiate sleep.

**P-053** [CONFIRMED — AUD-004]
**Evening light protocol:** Reduce blue light; shift to amber, yellow, or red light as the evening progresses. The goal is to allow melatonin to build while remaining active. This is the light-side of the evening polarity pair.

**P-054** [CONFIRMED — AUD-004]
**Pre-sleep routine is personalisable** around the core framework. Exercise, yoga, meditation, sounds, smells can all be added. They are optional layers on top of the non-negotiable environmental framework (light and temperature polarity).

**P-055** [CONFIRMED — AUD-004]
**Post-sleep routine is especially critical for PMers.** They are waking in an AMer-structured world — the serotonin trigger from light and the full morning sequence are even more important to compensate for the phase delay. The routine is the same; its importance is amplified for PMers.

**P-056** [STRONG INFERENCE — AUD-004]
**Preparing to sleep = downloading the day.** Nick describes reaching sleep time feeling "prepared" — having "downloaded my day, done everything I want to do, kept the pressure off that period." The daily structure (MRM, CRP) is what enables this psychological readiness. Without it, the mind carries unprocessed load into the sleep cycles.

## 4. Behavioural Patterns Extracted

**BP-023** [CONFIRMED — AUD-004]
**Overheated bedroom:** Most people want a warm, toasty bedroom with heavy bedding. This prevents the body-to-room temperature differential that signals sleep onset. Nick calls this out explicitly as a common and counterproductive pattern.

**BP-024** [CONFIRMED — AUD-004]
**Bright light in the evening:** Continuing to use full white/blue light into the evening suppresses melatonin and delays sleep onset. People don't realise they are actively fighting their own biology.

**BP-025** [CONFIRMED — AUD-004]
**Expecting sleep without preparation:** "Why do we wake up in the morning and just expect that whatever we've been doing all day long, we're going to be able to put right just before we go to bed?" The entire day is the preparation for sleep. Skipping this understanding means relying on the last 30 minutes to undo 16 hours of poor conditions.

## 5. Decision Rules Extracted

**DR-027** [CONFIRMED — AUD-004]
**Morning: move from dark to light and warm to cool** within the first 90-minute block after waking. This is the non-negotiable environmental sequence. Light exposure comes first and triggers everything else.

**DR-028** [CONFIRMED — AUD-004]
**Evening: shift to amber/yellow/red light** as the evening progresses — especially in the hour(s) before sleep. Eliminate or minimise blue/white light sources during this window.

**DR-029** [CONFIRMED — AUD-004]
**Bedroom temperature: keep the room a couple of degrees cooler than your body.** Achieve the body-warming needed with a shower, light yoga, or brief exercise before bed. Do not rely on a warm room to feel comfortable — that defeats the temperature trigger.

**DR-030** [CONFIRMED — AUD-004]
**Use the pre-sleep period to "download the day"** — resolve unfinished items mentally, avoid starting new demanding tasks, allow thoughts to settle. The goal is to arrive at sleep with a clear cognitive load, not processing half the day's residue.

**DR-031** [STRONG INFERENCE — AUD-004]
**For PMers: take the post-sleep routine even more seriously.** The serotonin trigger from morning light is the primary tool for compensating for the phase delay. Skipping it extends the PMer's slow activation even further into the morning.
