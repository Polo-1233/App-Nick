## Key Content

### Recovery = Activity Balance

- Nick's central framing: "the balance between mental and physical activity and mental and physical recovery" — trying to do too much leads to burnout/breakdown.
- Recovery is "as important as any physical or mental activity you might adopt."
- The lesson reframes CRP and MRM as **performance tactics**, not rest-by-default.
- **"If you want to achieve everything you want every day, these have to be vital."**

### CRP Mechanics (Lesson 5 version)

- CRP = 30-minute period (30% of a 90-minute cycle, not a full cycle).
- Timing: midday or early evening.
- **Does not require sleep**: "We're not trying to go to sleep. We're just trying to take some time out to give our brains and ourselves some nice mental and physical recovery."
- It is "mind space time, mindset" — mental disengagement, not necessarily physiological sleep.
- Analogy: sitting by a riverbank puts everything in perspective — the same reset effect is what a CRP delivers.
- Practical examples of what a CRP can look like: taking a dog for a walk, going to get water from a kitchen instead of from a desk, simply walking outside and back in again.
- Nick distinguishes the CRP clearly from napping-as-laziness: it's "part of these circadian rhythms, part of our chronotype, part of sleeping polyphasically rather than just monophasically."
- The 30-minute limit is deliberate: avoids rolling into a full 90-min cycle (which would be too long for a midday rest period).

### MRM Mechanics

- MRM = little mental and physical distractions every 90 minutes.
- Like the CRP, these do not require sleep — they are mindspace moments.
- The brain naturally wants to "micro sleep" in the right conditions (nodding on a train, drifting at a desk). MRMs create the structural space for this without forcing it.
- Key quote: "Creating these little moments throughout that 24-hour period using this R90 technique to ensure that we're able to give our brain the opportunity to take that recovery if it wants."

### Light Therapy Tools

- Natural daylight is the ideal — but not always accessible.
- Light therapy tools (lamps, desk lights) are a legitimate proxy: "bringing in technology and devices to help us with that process."
- Applications: morning post-sleep period (reset); desk lamp during the day (maintain phase 1/2 alertness); seasonal use (winter / DST-affected regions).
- Visualisation, sounds, and images of nature are additional sensory tools for inducing zone-out.

### Graveyard Spot

- Nick names the midday fatigue dip the "graveyard spot in business" — a known phenomenon in meetings and presentations.
- This is the body's natural CRP signal. The lesson teaches clients to use it rather than fight it.

### Synthesis of Lessons 1–4

- Nick explicitly frames this lesson as a consolidation: "Let's go even deeper... using the techniques that we've gone from lessons 1, 2, 3, 4, and now 5."
- CRPs and MRMs "all add up to protect you all year round from wasting valuable time sleeping without benefits."
- Goal: "a better balance between activity and recovery to protect you from everything you eat and drink and exercise."
