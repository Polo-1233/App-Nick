## 2. Main Ideas

1. "Eight hours" is a myth — no one actually sleeps 8 hours straight through
2. The clinical unit of sleep measurement is the 90-minute cycle, not total hours
3. Target: 5 cycles per day (= 7.5h); can be split across night and daytime recovery periods
4. Weekly target: 35 cycles (5/day × 7 days) — the real resilience metric
5. The consistent wake time is the immovable anchor; sleep times flex around it
6. CRP (Controlled Recovery Period) = 20–30 min nap, midday or 4–7pm
7. Every 90 minutes during the day: take a micro-recovery or distraction break

## 3. R90 Principles Extracted

**P-034** [CONFIRMED — AUD-003]
**The "8 hours" myth is debunked.** Nick has "never met anybody who actually sleeps for eight hours solidly all the way through." Eight hours is a cultural target, not a biological standard. The 90-minute cycle is the correct unit.

**P-035** [CONFIRMED — AUD-003]
**5 × 90-minute cycles = 7.5 hours** — this is the R90 daily target. It rounds to the "8 hours" conventional wisdom but is structured differently: it can be split across night sleep and daytime recovery periods.

**P-036** [CONFIRMED — AUD-003]
**Weekly accounting: 35 cycles per week** (5/day × 7 days). This is the resilience metric. A short night (3–4 cycles) is not a crisis — it can be balanced within the week using CRP. Nick explicitly plans across weeks, not just nights.

**P-037** [CONFIRMED — AUD-003]
**The consistent wake time is the anchor.** It does not change — not on weekends, not when you sleep shorter. "The sun doesn't change, so we can move the sleep times but not the wake time." Everything is counted backwards from this fixed point.

**P-038** [CONFIRMED — AUD-003]
**CRP = Controlled Recovery Period.** A deliberate 20–30 minute rest/nap taken either at midday or in the early evening (4–7pm window). This is not casual — it is a planned cycle that counts toward the daily/weekly total. Nick rebrands the "nap" concept to remove stigma: "let's stop using that word because it gives us an impression that we're going to go to sleep somewhere and it's for losers."

**P-039** [CONFIRMED — AUD-003]
**The midday "mind space moment"** = a lighter version of the CRP, aligned with the natural circadian dip. Not necessarily sleep — just a mental recovery pause. Nick uses this alongside the full CRP.

**P-040** [CONFIRMED — AUD-003]
**Every 90 minutes throughout the day:** take a small distraction/recovery break. This is automatic — you don't have to think about it. It is part of the overall polyphasic recovery approach, keeping the brain from accumulating uninterrupted pressure.

**P-041** [CONFIRMED — AUD-003]
**~30% of the 24-hour period in sleep state** = the R90 target. 30% × 24h = 7.2h ≈ 7.5h = 5 cycles. This framing reinforces that sleep is not separate from the day — it is a proportion of the total 24h cycle.

**P-042** [CONFIRMED — AUD-003]
Nick gives a live example using his own schedule:
- Wake: 6:30 (AMer, consistent)
- Day chopped into 90-min blocks from 6:30
- Option A (5 cycles): sleep 21:00–06:30 (7.5h nocturnal)
- Option B (4 cycles + CRP): CRP early evening → nocturnal sleep 00:30–06:30 (4 cycles, 6h) + 1 CRP
- Weekly flexibility: balance short nights with CRP to reach 35 cycles

**P-043** [CONFIRMED — AUD-003]
The **first 90 minutes after waking** is itself a recovery/activation block — not dead time. It requires light, hydration, fuel, mental challenges. This is the post-sleep period from DOC-002, now framed explicitly as a 90-minute block.

## 4. Behavioural Patterns Extracted

**BP-017** [CONFIRMED — AUD-003]
**Eight-hour fixation:** People base their entire sleep identity on "I need 8 hours." This creates anxiety when they don't hit it and fails to account for cycle quality or daytime recovery. R90 replaces this with cycle counting.

**BP-018** [CONFIRMED — AUD-003]
**CRP stigma:** "Nap" is socially coded as weakness or laziness in many cultures. Nick explicitly reframes it as a professional recovery tool — a Controlled Recovery Period — to remove the behavioural barrier.

**BP-019** [CONFIRMED — AUD-003]
**Pressure on the evening:** Without daytime recovery, people feel forced to go to bed early to "get their hours." This creates anxiety and often backfires. CRP in early evening relieves this pressure and allows the evening to be enjoyed.

## 5. Decision Rules Extracted

**DR-018** [CONFIRMED — AUD-003]
**Fix your wake time. Never change it.** Move sleep onset times to get more or fewer cycles; keep the wake time immovable. This is the single most important structural decision in R90.

**DR-019** [CONFIRMED — AUD-003]
**Count in complete cycles (90-min units), not hours.** A 4.5h night (3 cycles) is better than a 5h night (incomplete 4th cycle broken mid-cycle). Always target complete cycles.

**DR-020** [CONFIRMED — AUD-003]
**Manage sleep across the week, not nightly.** If Monday is a 3-cycle night, plan a CRP on Tuesday. Track the 35-cycle weekly target, not nightly achievement. This removes night-to-night anxiety.

**DR-021** [CONFIRMED — AUD-003]
**Schedule CRP in the early evening window (4–7pm)** when a short night is anticipated or has occurred. This adds 1–2 cycles to the daily total and takes pressure off bedtime.

**DR-022** [CONFIRMED — AUD-003]
**Every 90 minutes during the day:** take a micro-break, even briefly. This is passive recovery — a small mental distraction — not a sleep. It supports the overall polyphasic recovery architecture.
